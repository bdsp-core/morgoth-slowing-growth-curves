# Normative growth curves for EEG slowing across the lifespan and sleep–wake states: deviation-from-normal scoring that recapitulates expert report language

*First-draft manuscript. Numbers are drawn from the project's results and methods documents; placeholders marked `[TBD: …]` indicate values not yet available in the source material and required before submission.*

**Target venue:** clinical neurophysiology / digital-medicine (e.g., *Clinical Neurophysiology*, *Brain Communications*, *npj Digital Medicine*), accompanied by an open-source Python package and a published per-recording label set.

---

## Abstract

**Background.** Clinicians judge electroencephalographic (EEG) slowing — the core, everyday sign of both focal and generalized cerebral dysfunction — against expectations that depend strongly on the patient's age and on the brain state (wake versus sleep stage) at the time of recording. Those expectations rest on small, historical, largely qualitative normative studies. Existing quantitative lifespan EEG work characterizes *normal* values rather than *deviation from normal*, is almost always confined to the waking resting state, and is rarely sex- or sleep-stage-specific.

**Methods.** We assembled 12,379 routine clinical EEG recordings from approximately 12,000 patients spanning infancy to more than 90 years of age, drawn from routine practice at a single academic health system. We built a fully reproducible Python feature-extraction pipeline (referential-to-bipolar re-montaging, automated artifact rejection, multitaper spectral estimation, band powers, delta/alpha and theta/alpha ratios [DAR, TAR], and left–right homologous-channel asymmetry), validated against a prior precomputed feature set (per-band log-power r = 0.89–0.95) and calibrated to a physiological delta band (1–4 Hz). We assigned a sleep stage to every 15-second segment with a deep-learning stager, then estimated age × sex × stage percentile "growth curves" for each feature. Each recording is scored as deviation-from-normal via segment z-scores, prevalence, conditional severity, burden, persistence (runs and episodes), and stage-accentuation. A three-tier gate built on the expert-calibrated Morgoth EEG foundation model decides *whether* and *what* to report (normal → slowing → focal versus generalized); our normative features supply the descriptive detail. Generated clinician-style sentences and quantitative flags were validated against the free-text clinical reports.

**Results.** The curves reproduced known development and aging (absolute delta falls steeply through childhood to a plateau by ~30 years; normal-referenced z centers on 0 in normals). Stage-specific norms quantified the expected physiology: normal relative delta rises with sleep depth (W ≈ N1 < N2 < N3, REM intermediate). Age- and sex-adjusted discrimination was led by TAR, delta and theta power, and homologous asymmetry (AUCs ~0.67–0.73; TAR whole-head AUC 0.727 for normal vs generalized; per-channel T3–T5 delta asymmetry AUC 0.696 for focal). Our deviation features tracked the expert-calibrated detector (Pearson r ≈ 0.64–0.69; distillation R² ≈ 0.46). Generated descriptions agreed with the reports on band 0.74; localization, evaluated as gated per-axis tasks rather than lumped labels, was strong for focal lateralization (left-vs-right AUROC 0.87, band-matched) and temporal-lobe localization (F1 0.64), modest for generalized anterior-vs-posterior predominance (AUROC 0.66), and data-limited for rarer posterior foci. Gate detection was age-dependent (abnormal-vs-normal AUROC 0.79 in children to 0.95 in older adults; report-flag AUCs abnormal 0.90, focal 0.79, generalized 0.75).

**Conclusions.** To our knowledge this is the first large-scale, lifespan- and sleep-stage-resolved, deviation-from-normal instrument for EEG slowing, whose outputs are expressed in — and validated against — the language of clinical EEG reports. We release an open, reproducible package and a per-recording label set.

## 1. Introduction

Slowing of the EEG background is the most common and most clinically consequential abnormality a neurophysiologist reports. Focal slowing points to a localized structural or functional lesion; generalized slowing signals diffuse encephalopathy. Yet whether a given rhythm counts as "slow" is inescapably relative: the posterior dominant rhythm that is normal at 4 Hz in an infant would be markedly abnormal in an adult, and delta activity that is pathological in the waking adult is entirely physiological in deep sleep. Interpretation is therefore age- and state-dependent, and in practice remains qualitative and expert-dependent — a source of inter-reader variability and a barrier to scalable, reproducible second reads.

**What is already known.** The direction of lifespan spectral change is textbook-settled: low-frequency (delta, theta) power dominates in infancy and early childhood and declines with maturation while faster rhythms increase, with the posterior dominant rhythm accelerating from ~3–4 Hz in infancy toward the adult 8–12 Hz alpha by adolescence; aperiodic (1/f) activity flattens with development. In aging the picture is subtler and health-dependent, and much apparent "aging slowing" reflects comorbidity rather than healthy aging. Modern quantification has taken two forms. First, EEG "brain age" regresses chronological age on resting spectral (and aperiodic) features, formalized as a reusable M/EEG benchmark by Engemann et al. (2022, *NeuroImage*). Second, and conceptually closest to us, normative centile modeling of brain measures: Bethlehem et al. (2022, *Nature*) built MRI "brain charts" across the lifespan (n = 101,457) using GAMLSS to yield individual deviation scores — the structural-imaging analog of what we do functionally with EEG. Crucially, almost all lifespan qEEG reports *normal values*, not the *deviation of an individual clinical EEG from its matched norm*, and essentially none is sleep-stage-specific.

**The historical substrate clinicians still use.** Day-to-day EEG reading rests on age norms established decades ago on modest samples. Petersén & Eeg-Olofsson (1971, *Neuropädiatrie*) characterized the developing EEG in children aged 1–15 years (a widely cited N ≈ 700+), qualitatively-to-semiquantitatively and awake-focused. The most direct precedent for our deviation framing is John et al. (1980, *Science* 210:1255), whose "developmental equations" gave 32 linear age regressions of band power, replicated across US and Swedish children; the companion paper (1980, *Science* 210:1258) explicitly used deviation from these equations to flag dysfunction — an early "deviation-from-normal" idea, but over a narrow age band, eyes-closed rest only, and a few hundred subjects. John et al. (1988, *Science* 239:162), "Neurometrics," and the commercial normative databases it seeded (NeuroGuide/NxLink lineage) generalized age-regressed z-scoring, but these remain wake-resting, age-banded, of modest and somewhat opaque N, and not reproducible.

**Disease-specific abnormality indices.** A separate literature quantifies pathology directly without lifespan normalization. van Putten & Tavy (2004, *Stroke* 35:2489) introduced the Brain Symmetry Index (BSI), a 0–1 measure of interhemispheric spectral asymmetry correlating strongly with stroke severity (n = 21); the revised pairwise BSI followed (van Putten 2007, *Clin Neurophysiol* 118:2362). This is the intellectual ancestor of our homologous-channel asymmetry feature. Slowing ratios such as DAR and (delta+theta)/(alpha+beta) track acute-stroke severity (Finnigan & van Putten 2013), and relative delta/theta and alpha-delta ratios discriminate ICU delirium and coma. Every such instrument is tied to one disease and setting, uses fixed thresholds, and ignores age, sex, and — universally — sleep stage.

**Automated interpretation.** The Temple University Hospital Abnormal corpus (TUAB; López et al. 2015/16; Obeid & Picone 2016) established binary normal/abnormal classification as the standard benchmark, with ConvNets reaching ~85% (Schirrmeister et al. 2017; Gemein et al. 2020). EEG foundation models (BENDR, LaBraM, and clinically grounded variants) now dominate representation learning; our Morgoth heads sit in this family but are used as a calibrated *gate* rather than an end-to-end black box. Report NLP has extracted findings from free text (Biswal et al. 2015, *IEEE EMBC*, senior author Westover), and recent systems generate narrative from signal — but none ties generated findings to a lifespan-normative deviation model, nor validates stage-specific slowing sentences against the actual report corpus.

**The gap.** No prior work combines (a) lifespan-continuous, (b) sex-specific, and (c) sleep-stage-specific normative modeling of clinical slowing features with (d) per-recording deviation-from-normal scoring, (e) at the scale of ~12,000 patients from routine practice, and (f) closes the loop to clinician-style narrative validated against real reports. A full comparison against prior work is given in **Table T2** (from the literature review). (Caveat: exact Ns and page-level attributions for the historical norms should be re-verified before submission.)

**Contributions.** This paper delivers: (1) reproducible age × sex × sleep-stage normative growth curves for clinical slowing features across the lifespan; (2) a deviation-from-normal scoring framework (z, burden, prevalence, persistence, stage-accentuation, homologous asymmetry); (3) a Morgoth-gated focal/generalized decision coupling a self-supervised foundation model to interpretable norms; (4) automatic generation of clinician-style report sentences validated against free-text reports; and (5) an open Python package with a published per-recording label set.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Cohort and data

The study uses the *Growth_curves* dataset of routine clinical EEGs curated from a single academic health system (MGB sites), comprising 12,379 recordings from approximately 12,000 patients. Each recording carries report-derived structured finding flags (normal, abnormal, focal slowing, generalized slowing), which are **non-exclusive** — a report may note more than one. A key correction in this revision concerns the *normal reference*: because a study called normal cannot, by definition, harbor pathological slowing, we define controls as **clean normal** — flagged normal and *not* abnormal/focal/generalized — yielding n ≈ 2,501, rather than the naive "any-normal-flag" group (n ≈ 5,877) that a prior priority-cascade produced and which we found was **~57% contaminated** with recordings also carrying a slowing flag (their inclusion biased the normative curves toward more slowing). Slowing groups (focal n ≈ 2,742; generalized n ≈ 5,720, with substantial overlap) are evaluated **one-vs-clean-normal** rather than as a forced multiclass. We also corrected two label-extraction defects found during analysis (§2.8): a structured-flag under-count (findings encoded "verified"/"annotation" rather than "report" were dropped, ~8% of focal / ~5% of generalized) and a broken report-text side/region extractor. Labels ultimately derive from the interpreting clinician's report. *(Counts are being finalized on the full-cohort re-run.)* Cohort demographics are summarized in **Table T1**. The median age across all recordings is 53.0 years (Q1–Q3 26.0–69.0). Sex was resolved from OMOP (`gender_concept_id`) for 12,378 of 12,379 recordings and is balanced at 49.5% female / 50.5% male overall. Eighteen recordings with implausible ages (<0 or >120 years) were excluded. Importantly, controls skew roughly 20 years younger than the slowing groups (control median 38.0 years versus focal 61.0 and generalized 58.0), and 75+ controls are relatively sparse (n = 442) — an age confound that motivates conditioning all scoring on age and sex. Age is taken from the recording datetime encoded in each filename (`sub-<BDSP_ID>_<YYYYMMDDHHMMSS>`) combined with the OMOP birth date; sex from OMOP. Reproducible cohort metadata (`metadata/cohort_metadata.csv`) regenerates Table T1 and the curves without S3 access.

### 2.2 Reproducible feature extraction

The original *Growth_curves* features were precomputed in MATLAB by a collaborator, but that code is unavailable. To make the pipeline reproducible and extensible to new recordings, we reimplemented extraction in Python (`features/extract.py`; `scripts/13_recompute_features.py` regenerated all 12,379 recordings). The pipeline re-montages the referential 10-20 EEG to 18 bipolar (double-banana) channels, applies a 0.5 Hz high-pass and 50/60 Hz notch, segments into 15-second windows (3000 samples at 200 Hz, step 2800), and computes a multitaper power spectral density (time-bandwidth NW = 4, 7 tapers). Per segment we derive 31 features — delta/theta/alpha/beta/gamma power, total power, relative powers, and inter-band ratios (including DAR = delta/alpha and TAR = theta/alpha) — for each of the 18 bipolar channels, plus 8 homologous left–right pairs for focal localization. Channels are aggregated into regions (whole-head, left/right temporal, left/right parasagittal, midline) via `config/channels_regions.yaml`. Linear powers are log-transformed before z-scoring.

**Validation.** We validated the Python features against the original precomputed set (`scripts/12_validate_extractor.py`): per-band log-power correlations were r = 0.89–0.95 (delta 0.89, theta 0.90, alpha 0.89, beta 0.95, total 0.87; gamma 0.79, which is filtering-sensitive). Downstream science was preserved: stage physiology (normal median relative delta N3 > N2 > W/REM > N1) and discrimination both reproduced, and the newly added per-channel homologous asymmetry became a top focal discriminator (see §3.4).

**Calibration.** Because relative power depends on the low-frequency edge and total-band definition, uncalibrated relative delta ran high (normal whole-head median ~0.5–0.6 versus a physiological reference near 0.30). We calibrated the delta band to 1–4 Hz, bringing normal whole-head relative delta to 0.34 (physiological). The full pipeline was recomputed after calibration and all validation metrics held (report agreement region 0.92 / side 0.79 / band 0.74; LR↔Morgoth r 0.64–0.69; discrimination unchanged). Because all deviation scoring is computed as z relative to each feature's own age/sex/stage normal curve, the scoring, discrimination, and generated descriptions are scale-invariant and were unaffected by this calibration; only raw relative-power magnitudes changed.

**Artifact rejection.** Norms and reports must reflect clean brain signal, not flat or contaminated segments. A per-segment artifact filter (`features/artifact.py`) marks a 15-second bipolar segment unusable if it is flat/disconnected (median channel peak-to-peak < 1 µV, or >50% of channels flat), high-amplitude (any channel peak-to-peak > 500 µV, e.g. movement or electrode pop), or EMG-dominated (fraction of 1–45 Hz power above 20 Hz > 0.55); the usable-segment fraction is carried into scoring so low-yield recordings are flagged rather than silently scored on a handful of segments. On a real full-length BIDS EDF that was largely disconnected, rejection kept 93/253 segments (37%) and recovered a physiological whole-head relative delta of 0.32 from an artifact-inflated 0.047 (`docs/artifact_rejection_plan.md`). Thresholds are provisional pending manual-review calibration.

**Full-recording ingestion.** The same extractor runs unchanged on full multi-hour recordings through a validated ingestion path (`io/edf.py`) that harmonizes heterogeneous BIDS EDFs (variable sampling rate, 30–50 channels, old/new 10-20 nomenclature) to the 19 referential channels at 200 Hz, then applies artifact rejection, whole-recording staging, and per-stage features. This pilot ran end-to-end with cohort-range relative delta and realistic W/N1/N2/N3/REM staging, establishing the route to lifespan-scale expansion — in particular the overnight/deep-sleep recordings needed to fill the adult N3 gap (a cloud-scale run is planned; `docs/aws_cloud_plan.md`).

### 2.3 Sleep staging

Routine EEG feature sets are unstaged (all segments marked "Other"). We staged each recording from the raw EEG with the morgoth2 deep-learning stager (window-level 5-class model `ss_hm_1.pth`; the architecturally identical `SLEEPPSG.pth` serves as substitute), run on Apple MPS. The model reads continuous raw segments (200 Hz) and emits per-second, 5-class (W/N1/N2/N3/REM) predictions at a 1-second step; we assign each 15-second feature segment its majority stage. Stage distributions were validated against expectations for routine clips (predominantly wake with drowsy/N1/N2) and cross-checked against a labeled sleep-staging set. The one operational constraint was Python-environment isolation (the stager's dependency `pyhealth` pins pandas <2, incompatible with the analysis stack), resolved with a dedicated staging virtual environment.

### 2.4 Normative growth curves

For each feature × region (and, in v2, × stage) we estimated normal-population percentile "growth curves" as a function of age within sex, using kernel-weighted quantiles over the control group. Curves were validated at the subject level (normal-referenced z centers on 0 for controls). Coverage and minimum-effective-n guards protect against thin cells: the fitter returns NaN below a minimum effective n (for example, N3 is truncated above ~age 34), and `n_eff` accompanies every curve. Curves are provided for 8 features × 5 regions (`figures/curves/`) and per stage (`figures/stage_curves/`, `figures/regional_stage_curves/`).

### 2.5 Deviation scoring

Each recording is scored as deviation from its matched norm. For every segment we compute a z-score against the age/sex/stage normal curve, then summarize at the recording level: **prevalence** (fraction of usable segments abnormal, mapped to ACNS-style frequency words rare→continuous), **conditional severity** (magnitude when present, mapped to mild/moderate/marked), **burden** (combining prevalence and severity), **persistence** (longest continuous run, number of episodes, median episode duration), **stage-accentuation** (the stage with maximum per-stage burden; whether present only in sleep), and **homologous asymmetry** (left–right z on homologous pairs, for lateralization). A patient-level empirical-percentile z summarizes overall deviation. Because every segment is scored against *its own stage's* normal curve, delta that is abnormal in wake but normal in N2/N3 yields near-zero prevalence in normals.

### 2.6 Topography and the Morgoth gate

Detection and description are deliberately separated (`docs/report_architecture.md`): the Morgoth foundation-model heads, calibrated against expert reports, decide *whether* and *what* to report; our normative features decide *how to describe* it. The gate is three-tier and hierarchical. **Tier 1** (`NORMAL.pth` + EEG-level aggregator) asks whether the EEG is abnormal at all; if normal, the pipeline says so and stops. **Tier 2** (`SLOWING.pth`) asks whether pathological slowing specifically is present, with high specificity to expert reporting so slowing is not over-called on normative deviation alone; only this tier gates our slowing description. **Tier 3** (`FOC_SLOWING_EEGlevel.pth`, `GEN_SLOWING_EEGlevel.pth`) sets the topographic branch(es); focal and generalized are separate, non-mutually-exclusive heads, so multifocal/mixed cases can fire both. Each head is two-stage: a window-level model reading raw data, then an EEG-level aggregator producing one probability per recording (`scripts/14_aggregate_gate.py`; outputs described in `docs/morgoth_gate_outputs.md`). Consistency between the gate and our features is itself a signal: when Morgoth calls focal but asymmetry z ≈ 0, confidence is lowered and morphology review is recommended.

### 2.7 Report generation

Once a branch is gated in, a sentence is templated from the kept features (`scripts/16_gated_report.py`): location (region + side from regional burden and asymmetry z), band/type (delta vs theta vs mixed; pathological slowing usually defaults to mixed unless one band clearly dominates), severity (patient-level z), prevalence/intermittency (ACNS words), persistence (longest run, episodes), and state-dependence (present in wake vs only in sleep; the most-accentuated stage). For generalized slowing, low relative alpha/theta adds "paucity of faster activity."

### 2.8 Validation and statistics

We validated at three levels. **Against Morgoth** (`scripts/17`): agreement of our deviation-based logistic-regression probability with Morgoth probabilities (Pearson/Spearman) and distillation R² of our features onto Morgoth probabilities. **Against report flags** (`scripts/19`): AUC of Morgoth and of our scores against report-derived abnormal/focal/generalized flags. **Against report text** (`scripts/20`): agreement of our generated statement with the report where the report states region, side, or band. Discrimination (`scripts/06`) reports age- and sex-adjusted AUCs of each feature's normal-referenced z between groups; feature selection (`scripts/15`) combines L1-logistic (LASSO), random-forest importance, and bootstrap stability selection with correlation clustering to yield a parsimonious, de-duplicated keep-list, distilling both the clinical label and Morgoth's abnormality call.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Cohort and coverage

The cohort spans infancy to >90 years (**Table T1**). All nine age bands are represented, with the largest counts at 60–74 (n = 3,027) and 45–59 (n = 2,329) and the smallest in early childhood (3–5: n = 321). Sex is balanced within every group. The control group is younger than the slowing groups, reinforcing the need for age/sex conditioning. Coverage by sleep stage × age (`docs/coverage_by_stage.md`) shows that wake, N1, and N2 are well powered across the whole lifespan in normals (mostly hundreds per age band, minimum 73), so curves for these stages are trustworthy including the tails. N3 is under-powered from age ≥3 (154 recordings at 0–2 years, then 13–42 per band) — partly physiological (N3 declines with age) and partly a sampling limitation of short routine clips — so adult N3 curves have wide confidence intervals and are truncated by the min-n guard. REM is adequate in adults but thin at the extremes (62 at 0–2; 69 at 75+). Abnormal cases are too sparse per stage × age cell for stage-specific validation (often 0–5 per cell), so stage-specific discrimination is treated as exploratory and validation is performed stage-pooled on the well-powered groups (2,067 focal / 5,396 generalized).

### 3.2 Growth curves reproduce development and aging

The normative curves recapitulate textbook maturation. Absolute delta power falls steeply through childhood to a plateau by approximately 30 years (**Figure F1**; `figures/curves/log_delta__whole_head.png`), consistent with the well-established slow-to-fast developmental shift. Normal-referenced z-scores center on 0 for controls across the lifespan, confirming that the curves are well-calibrated to the normal population. Curves for all 8 features × 5 regions are provided in `figures/curves/`.

### 3.3 Sleep-stage-specific norms

Stage-resolved curves quantify the expected physiology of slowing across sleep depth. In normals, median relative delta rises with sleep depth: **W ≈ N1 < N2 < N3**, with REM intermediate (**Figure F2**; `figures/stage_curves/rel_delta__whole_head.png`). The direct clinical consequence is that delta which is abnormal in wake is entirely normal in N2/N3; because each segment is scored against its own stage's normal curve, normal prevalence approaches zero even in sleep-heavy recordings. Regional stage-specific curves (`figures/regional_stage_curves/`) extend this per region.

An initial obstacle was that the original cohort's sleep staging had been run only on the normative (normal) recordings, leaving the abnormal recordings unstaged — so a stage-stratified *abnormal-versus-normal* analysis was not possible. We corrected this by staging the abnormal recordings directly from their archived raw clips (`scripts/36`), yielding staged focal and generalized cases. A stage-stratified detection analysis (whole-head summed deviation of rel_delta/DAR/TAR from age-matched normals, abnormal vs normal AUROC per age band; **Figure F2b**, `results/age_auroc_by_stage.csv`) then shows that this simple spectral score is **modest and age-dependent in every stage** (AUROC ≈ 0.44 in youth rising to ≈ 0.70 in older adults, similar across W/N2/N3/REM). Notably the whole-head average dilutes focal (localized) slowing, so recording-level detection with the full feature set is far stronger than this per-stage whole-head score — the stage-resolved curves are most useful for *normalizing physiology*, not as a stand-alone detector.

### 3.4 Which features discriminate

Age- and sex-adjusted AUCs (`results/discrimination.md`; **Table T3**, **Figure F3**) identify a small, clinically interpretable discriminative set. For **normal vs focal**, TAR led (L_temporal AUC 0.704, whole-head 0.697), followed closely by the per-channel homologous asymmetry `|asym_ch_T3–T5_delta|` (AUC 0.696) and temporal delta asymmetry (0.682); theta and delta power followed (log_theta whole-head 0.681; log_delta whole-head 0.658). The strong showing of homologous asymmetry validates the per-channel/homologous-pair features as focal discriminators. For **normal vs generalized**, TAR again led (whole-head 0.727; R_parasagittal 0.725; L_temporal 0.725), with DAR next (L_temporal 0.685) and delta power behind (whole-head 0.659). Distinguishing **focal from generalized** by spectral deviation alone was weak (best AUCs ~0.55–0.58, e.g., DAR R_parasagittal 0.576), consistent with topography rather than spectral shape being the discriminating axis — which is exactly what the Morgoth gate supplies. Age/sex adjustment mattered: adjusted AUCs consistently exceeded age-confounded raw AUCs (e.g., log_theta whole-head 0.681 adjusted vs 0.584 raw).

Feature selection (`results/feature_selection.md`) combining L1-logistic, random-forest importance, and bootstrap stability under correlation clustering converged on a compact keep-list — **TAR (θ/α), DAR (δ/α), log-δ, and log-θ** (whole-head plus temporal/parasagittal), all with stability 1.0 — plus per-channel homologous asymmetry (e.g., T3–T5 delta) for focal localization. The same features rose to the top whether the target was the expert label or a distillation of Morgoth's abnormality call (e.g., TAR whole-head RF importance 0.111 for the Morgoth-abnormal target), indicating that our interpretable features reconstruct much of the expert-matched detector.

### 3.5 Deviation features versus the expert-calibrated Morgoth

Sanity checks confirmed the gate behaves: median Morgoth p_abnormal was 0.10 in normals versus 0.98–0.996 in the focal/generalized groups, p_focal was highest in focal cases, and p_generalized highest in generalized. Our objective, age/sex-deviation logistic regression tracked Morgoth closely (`results/lr_vs_morgoth.md`): for p_abnormal, Pearson r = 0.693 (Spearman ρ = 0.690) with distillation R² = 0.456; for p_slowing, r = 0.635 (ρ = 0.694), R² = 0.458. Against the clinical label directly, our deviation-based LR actually reached AUC 0.962 versus Morgoth 0.921 — our features are highly sensitive to the label — but the correlation of ~0.64–0.69 shows they capture much, though not all, of what the expert-calibrated detector encodes. The probability scatter (**Figure F4**; `figures/lr_vs_morgoth.png`) makes the difference concrete: our LR is over-confident, pushing probabilities toward 0 and 1, whereas the report-calibrated Morgoth is better spread — consistent with high label-sensitivity but only moderate probability agreement, and a reason to keep Morgoth as the gate. ROC and precision–recall curves for abnormal detection by both scores are shown in **Figure F3** (`figures/roc_prc/roc.png`, `figures/roc_prc/prc.png`). Operated as a slowing gate at P ≥ 0.30 (Youden J = 0.75), Morgoth gated in 79% of focal, 86% of generalized, and only 8.9% of normal recordings.

Gate discrimination is **age-dependent** (**Figure F4b**, `results/age_auroc.csv`): abnormal-vs-normal AUROC rises from **0.79 in children (0–12)** to **0.95 in older adults (61–75)**, dipping slightly in the very elderly (0.94 at 76–100); focal detection is strong at all ages (0.97–0.99) while generalized climbs 0.88 → 0.96. The weaker pediatric performance is a concrete reporting caveat — automated slowing detection should be applied more cautiously in children — and reflects both developmental spectral variability and thinner pediatric training coverage.

### 3.6 Agreement with clinical reports

We matched cohort recordings to their free-text clinical reports (11,339 / 12,379 matched to report-derived flags; 12,303 / 12,379 matched for text-extracted labels), extracted labels with provenance, and validated (`results/report_agreement.md`; **Table T4**). Raw report text is not published — only derived labels, with pointers back to the source notes. For **report-flag agreement**, Morgoth tracked the reports strongly: abnormal AUC 0.904, focal 0.785, generalized 0.745; our deviation-based LR was close behind at 0.751 / 0.631 / 0.647, establishing face validity. For **report-text agreement** — our generated statement versus the report where the report states the finding — raw agreement was region 0.92 (n = 407), side 0.79 (n = 5,406), and **band 0.74** (n = 5,196). The region and side figures are, however, **inflated by class imbalance** and must be read with per-class metrics: 92% of located reports are temporal and 83% of lateralized reports are bilateral, so a system that predicts "temporal/bilateral" by default scores highly on raw agreement while never identifying the other regions (per-class F1 ≈ 0 for frontal/parietal/occipital; left/right F1 0.35/0.24). We therefore evaluated localization honestly with **macro-F1** (equal weight per region) across three predictors (**Table T4b**, `results/region_supervised.md`): the temporal-default macro-F1 = 0.16; a max-deviation-lobe rule = 0.12; and a supervised classifier on age-adjusted per-channel deviations = **0.23** (best), with temporal (F1 0.54) and frontal (0.32) reasonably localizable but parietal/occipital still poor (F1 < 0.1, n ≈ 66 each). Localization is thus **partially solved** — strong for the common temporal/frontal foci, data-limited for rare posterior foci — and is a target for region-stratified data collection rather than a solved axis.

Crucially, **lateralization must be posed as a focal-gated, binary task.** Detection of focal slowing is side-*invariant* (it responds to the *magnitude* of homologous asymmetry), whereas lateralization needs its *sign*; evaluating side as a three-way label over all abnormal recordings is dominated by the bilateral majority (generalized slowing is bilateral by definition), which is why the naive side agreement looked weak. Restricting to focal recordings with a stated side and predicting **left vs. right** from signed homologous asymmetry (`results/lateralization_gated.md`, **Figure F4d**) gives **AUROC 0.866** (balanced accuracy 0.81; n = 555), with a single signed feature — temporal δ asymmetry — already at 0.856. Lateralization is therefore a *strong* axis once gated correctly; the operational design is to predict side only when the gate calls focal, and to abstain to "bilateral/uncertain" when the asymmetry magnitude is small. Lateralization is also **band-matched**: theta-predominant focal slowing is lateralized by theta asymmetry (AUROC 0.91) and delta by delta (0.90), with both for mixed — so the reported side is driven by the same band as the reported slowing. For **generalized** slowing (no side), an **anterior-vs-posterior** predominance axis (frontal FIRDA-like vs posterior OIRDA-like) is recoverable at AUROC ~0.66 (`results/region_gated.md`). Focal **lobe** localization, evaluated focal-gated, is strong for temporal (F1 0.64) but data-limited for rarer frontal/central/posterior foci. Band was initially the weak axis (0.33): recognizing that pathological slowing is usually *mixed* and defaulting to mixed unless one band clearly dominates raised band agreement from 0.33 to 0.74, while side and region were already strong. Band now appears near its ceiling for spectral-only features — 0.74 approaches the ~74% base rate of "mixed" slowing in the reports, so a band-power model that defaults to mixed can gain little more without morphology. A pilot morphology feature (slow-band spectral centroid) did not improve the band call (0.45 vs 0.74) because the centroid is dominated by the 1/f background (~2.1 Hz for both delta and mixed segments); aperiodic (FOOOF) decomposition is the indicated next step (`docs/morphology_features.md`). All three axes now have good face validity against experts, and all labels (Morgoth probabilities, report-derived flags, and report-text-extracted band/side/region) are published per-recording with provenance.

### 3.7 Example generated reports

The final gated pipeline (`results/example_reports_final.md`; gate at Morgoth P(slowing) ≥ 0.302) produces clinician-style sentences that combine the gate's whether/what decision with our normative description. Representative focal and generalized exemplars, including stage-dependence (**Box 1**):

> *Frequent mild left temporal mixed theta/delta slowing — present in 48% of segments; peak 2.0 SD above age/stage-matched norms; longest run 3.8 min over 4 episodes.* (age 50 F, focal)

> *Frequent mild generalized mixed theta/delta slowing — present in 19% of segments; peak 1.3 SD above age/stage-matched norms; longest run 0.7 min over 6 episodes; present only during sleep; accentuated in N1.* (age 38 F, generalized)

> *Frequent mild left parasagittal mixed theta/delta slowing … peak 2.3 SD above age/stage-matched norms … accentuated in N3.* (age 71 M, focal)

> *Abundant mild generalized mixed theta/delta slowing — present in 57% of segments … present only during sleep; accentuated in N2.* (age 81 F, generalized)

Normal recordings that clear the gate generally yield "No pathological slowing." The examples also surface the known failure mode (§4): some Morgoth-confident cases show flat spectral features ("peak ~0 SD"), where Morgoth detects a pattern our current spectral features do not, and the sentence reads weak.

## 4. Discussion

**What is new.** This work unifies four previously separate literatures into a single instrument. Unlike lifespan qEEG that reports *normal values*, we score *deviation from normal* per recording. Unlike the classic norms (Petersén & Eeg-Olofsson; John et al.), which are small-N, wake-only, age-banded, and — in the commercial lineage — opaque, our norms are lifespan-continuous, sex-specific, sleep-stage-resolved, built at the scale of ~12,000 patients, and fully reproducible in open Python. Unlike disease-specific abnormality indices (BSI, DAR, ICU signatures), which use fixed thresholds tied to one setting and ignore age/sex/stage, our features are normed against the matched normal population. And unlike binary classifiers (TUAB lineage) or ungrounded report generators, we produce localized, banded, graded, stage-aware descriptions validated against the actual report corpus. This extends the deviation idea of John et al. (1980/1988) to a modern scale and to slowing and asymmetry specifically, and it is the functional-EEG analog of the MRI "brain charts" of Bethlehem et al. (2022).

**Clinical implications.** The pipeline offers an objective, reproducible second read and a draft report. Its strongest axes are **whether/what** (abnormal detection, and band once defaulted to mixed) and **grading against age/stage-matched norms**; a handful of interpretable features (TAR, DAR, delta/theta power, homologous asymmetry) carry most of the discriminative signal. **Localization is more nuanced than raw agreement suggests** — the headline region/side numbers are inflated by the temporal/bilateral majority, and honest per-class evaluation (macro-F1 0.23) shows reliable localization only for the common temporal and frontal foci, with posterior foci data-limited — so topographic claims in the generated report should be surfaced with a confidence that reflects this. Stage-resolved norms let the system distinguish physiological sleep slowing from pathology automatically, something wake-only norms cannot do and a frequent source of over-reading. The published per-recording labels also constitute a reusable resource for benchmarking.

**Reports are a directionally biased reference, and a normative model can detect what they miss.** Clinical EEG reports are our reference standard, but they are an *imperfect reference with a known, directional bias*: because theta/delta slowing is a normal feature of sleep and "how much is too much" is hard to judge by eye, neurophysiologists systematically **under-declare slowing that occurs in sleep**, and generalized slowing is under-called relative to focal (in our report corpus, generalized-slowing sentences are ~1.5× more likely than focal to be qualified as drowsy/sleep-related, and a study with genuinely pathological sleep slowing is often signed out "normal overall"). We therefore treat concordance with reports as validation only *where reports are reliable* (focal, and awake findings), and pose a second, complementary hypothesis: that our **age × sex × stage** normative model is itself the better instrument in exactly the regime humans find hardest — generalized slowing in sleep — because it applies a population-calibrated threshold *per stage* (the normal band in N2/N3 is high and wide, so only excess beyond it is flagged). Because no independent gold standard exists, we make the "excess" detections credible three ways rather than one: **(1) dose-response** — deviation magnitude should rise monotonically across report strata (clean-normal < abnormal-unspecified < abnormal-with-slowing-named); **(2) a within-subject wake→sleep test** — studies whose report flags abnormality *in wake only* should also show elevated deviation *in their sleep stages*, evidence that the abnormality is present in sleep and detected there even when the reader did not call it; and **(3) convergent validity** — deviation should track independent abnormality markers (absent sleep spindles/K-complexes, abnormal posterior dominant rhythm, and the Morgoth gate). We frame human-missed detections as *candidate* abnormalities supported by this convergent evidence, and propose the definitive test — a blinded expert re-read of high-deviation report-normal sleep studies (or linkage to clinical outcomes) — as prospective validation. This reframes the contribution from "reproduce the report" to "a stage-aware normative complement that is most valuable where expert reading is weakest" (full plan in `docs/normative_deviation_plan.md`).

**Laterality of focal slowing — an age-dependent left predominance.** Among focal recordings with a stated side, the reported side is **left in ~74% overall, rising with age from ~0.65 (≤18 y) to ~0.77 (≥76 y)** — i.e., the well-described left-anterior-temporal predominance of intermittent temporal slowing is concentrated in older adults, consistent with the "temporal slowing of the elderly"/benign-aging literature and its proposed cerebrovascular basis (Inui et al., *Psychiatry Clin Neurosci* 2001; Meritam Larsen & Beniczky systematic review, *Seizure* 2025; Wüstenhagen et al. SCORE normal-variants, *Clin Neurophysiol Pract* 2022; older community cohorts Hughes & Cayaffa 1977, Oken & Kaye 1992). Whether this skew is fully biological versus partly methodological (montage/reference conventions, side-asymmetric artifact, greater scrutiny of the left anterior temporal chain) is unresolved and we do not adjudicate it. Two design consequences follow. First, because we want the system to report *whatever side is actually present* without inheriting this prevalence prior, we train and evaluate the lateralizer with **left↔right symmetry augmentation** (equivalently, an intercept-free antisymmetric model on signed asymmetry), which removes the prior while preserving discrimination (left/right recall equalized, AUROC unchanged; flip-consistency exact). Second, lateralization is driven by *within-subject* signed asymmetry, which is intrinsically age- and sex-controlled — and empirically its AUROC is stable across the lifespan (0.81–0.89) — so, unlike detection and regional deviation (both explicitly age×sex×stage-normed), the lateralizer needs no separate age model.

**Relationship to Morgoth.** The gate-and-describe design deliberately separates *detection* (Morgoth, calibrated to expert calls but unable to describe) from *description* (our interpretable, sensitive, but not-report-calibrated norms). The two are complementary: Morgoth decides whether and what to report, our features supply the adjectives and the quantitative parenthetical, and disagreement between them (e.g., Morgoth-focal with asymmetry z ≈ 0) is itself an actionable low-confidence flag. That some Morgoth-confident cases have flat spectral features points to the main descriptive limitation: our current features are spectral, and Morgoth evidently exploits morphological patterns (waveform shape, transients, rhythmicity) that band power does not capture. A first pilot in this direction — a slow-band spectral centroid intended to sharpen the band call — was negative (0.45 vs 0.74), because the centroid is dominated by the 1/f background (~2.1 Hz for both delta and mixed segments) rather than by any oscillatory peak. The lesson is that closing the gap needs features that separate periodic from aperiodic activity: aperiodic (FOOOF) exponent/offset, time-domain morphology (line length, amplitude, Hjorth parameters), and rhythmicity measures that distinguish polymorphic from rhythmic (FIRDA/TIRDA) slowing, together with distillation of Morgoth probabilities into interpretable features. This roadmap is scoped and prioritized in `docs/morphology_features.md`.

## 5. Limitations

Several limitations temper these results. **Absolute relative-power calibration** is approximate; although the delta band was recalibrated to 1–4 Hz (normal whole-head relative delta 0.34) and all z-based results are scale-invariant, raw relative-power magnitudes and cross-feature absolute comparisons should be read cautiously. **Band determination** remains the hardest axis (0.74) and currently defaults to "mixed"; finer band attribution will require morphology and better spectral resolution. **Morphology is not yet modeled** — a known cause of weak descriptions in some Morgoth-confident cases. **Region localization is only partially solved.** Honest per-class evaluation (macro-F1 0.23 for the best, supervised, predictor) shows reliable localization for temporal and frontal foci but weak performance for the rarer central/parietal/occipital foci (per-class F1 < 0.1, n ≈ 66 each); the high raw region-agreement (0.92) is a majority-class artifact of temporal predominance. Improving this needs both better features and **region-stratified data collection** to build an adequately powered off-temporal evaluation and training set.

**Coverage is uneven, and one gap was self-inflicted and now fixed.** Sleep staging in the source cohort had originally been run only on the normal recordings, so abnormal-with-sleep cells were empty and stage-stratified abnormal analyses were initially impossible; we corrected this by staging the abnormal recordings directly from their archived raw clips (`scripts/36`), and the stage-stratified analysis (§3.3) now uses real staged focal/generalized cases. Adult N3 remains the thinnest normal cell (wide CIs). Both gaps are further addressed by the validated full-recording ingestion pipeline (`io/edf.py` harmonization + `features/artifact.py` rejection + whole-recording staging and per-stage features), which folds in longer/overnight recordings from the broader repository; a cloud-scale run is prepared (`fleet/`, `docs/aws_cloud_plan.md`). **Single health system** limits generalizability; external validation is needed. **Label quality required active correction, and one concern remains open.** Auditing the labels during analysis revealed three problems, two now fixed: (i) the *normal reference was 57% contaminated* by recordings that also carried a slowing flag, because a priority cascade let "normal" win ties — corrected to a clean-normal reference (slowing/abnormal override normal), which lowered the normal whole-head relative-delta baseline (≈0.41→0.35) and makes every deviation score honest; (ii) the *report-text side/region extractor was badly under-calling laterality* — it scoped only to sentences containing "slow," checked "diffuse/generalized" before any explicit side, and ignored electrode names, dumping ~81% of slowing reports into "bilateral"; a rewrite (per-clause scoping, 10-20 electrode→side/region mapping, R>L/L>R predominance, side-wins-over-diffuse) roughly tripled recoverable unilateral sides and raised the focal lateralization set from ~555 to ~1,300–2,000 recordings (AUROC 0.87→0.93). The **open concern** is the *generalized-slowing flag itself*: it is positive in ~77% of the cohort, implausibly high for pathological generalized slowing, and almost certainly sweeps in physiologic/drowsy sleep slowing; because it is a whole-recording, stage-agnostic label it cannot by itself distinguish pathological from physiologic slowing — which is precisely why we lean on the per-stage normative model and the convergent-validity strategy above rather than on this flag as ground truth. **Report text is an imperfect and *directionally biased* ground truth** more broadly (see Discussion): completeness and phrasing vary, and sleep/generalized slowing is systematically under-reported, bounding — and in the sleep regime, likely *under*-stating — achievable agreement. Finally, **routine EEG is short**, limiting sleep capture and the stability of stage-specific per-recording estimates.

---

## 6. Conclusion

We present, to our knowledge, the first large-scale, lifespan- and sleep-stage-resolved, deviation-from-normal instrument for EEG slowing. Built on 12,379 routine clinical EEGs from ~12,000 patients, it reproduces known development and aging, quantifies the stage-dependence of physiological slowing (W ≈ N1 < N2 < N3), identifies a compact interpretable feature set (TAR, DAR, delta/theta power, homologous asymmetry), tracks an expert-calibrated foundation-model detector (r ≈ 0.64–0.69), and produces clinician-style report sentences that agree with free-text reports on region (0.92), side (0.79), and band (0.74). By coupling normative deviation scoring to a foundation-model gate and validating the output in the language of clinical reports, it moves beyond both un-normed abnormality indices and ungrounded automated interpretation. We release an open, reproducible Python package and a published per-recording label set to enable external validation and extension, particularly to adult deep-sleep norms and morphology-aware features.
