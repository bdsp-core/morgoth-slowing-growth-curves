## Abstract

**Background.** Clinicians judge electroencephalographic (EEG) slowing — the core, everyday sign of both focal and generalized cerebral dysfunction — against expectations that depend strongly on the patient's age and on the brain state (wake versus sleep stage) at the time of recording. Those expectations rest on small, historical, largely qualitative normative studies. Existing quantitative lifespan EEG work characterizes *normal* values rather than *deviation from normal*, is almost always confined to the waking resting state, and is rarely sex- or sleep-stage-specific.

**Methods.** We assembled **~27,000 clinical EEG recordings** — ~11,000 routine studies plus ~16,000 overnight/long-term recordings, from a single academic health system — spanning infancy to >90 years, and processed *every* recording through **one identical reproducible pipeline** (referential-to-bipolar re-montaging, automated artifact rejection, multitaper spectral estimation, band powers, delta/alpha and theta/alpha ratios [DAR, TAR], homologous-channel asymmetry; delta band 1–4 Hz). A deep-learning stager assigned a sleep stage to every 15-second segment; the overnight recordings fill the deep-sleep (N3) coverage that routine clips cannot. We estimated **lifespan × sleep-stage percentile "growth curves"** for each feature using GAMLSS/LMS (Box–Cox-t with age-varying skewness), on precise OMOP-derived fractional ages. **Sexes are pooled** — conditioning on sex changes abnormality discrimination by ΔAUROC ≤0.002 in every setting. "Normal" is the **union of both report-normal cohorts** (routine + overnight), a broad, conservative clinical reference that we show costs no detection power; a routine-vs-overnight comparison, plus a same-recording pipeline control, establishes that residual cohort differences are physiological (vigilance state + population), not computational. Each recording is scored as deviation-from-normal via segment z-scores, prevalence, conditional severity, burden, persistence, and stage-accentuation; a Morgoth foundation-model gate decides *whether/what* to report and our normative features supply the description, validated against free-text reports.

**Results.** The curves reproduced known development and aging (absolute delta falls steeply through childhood to a plateau by ~30 years) and quantified the stage physiology (relative delta rises with sleep depth W < N1 < N2 < N3, REM intermediate). **Correct labels matter more than any modelling choice.** The report-derived generalized-slowing class, as conventionally extracted, is contaminated by *physiologic* slowing: text such as *"generalized slowing, likely related to drowsiness"* in an otherwise normal study fires a naive extractor. Restricting the pathologic class to slowing the report **names among its abnormalities** reclassified **5,528 recordings** and lowered every detection estimate. On the corrected labels, age-adjusted whole-head deviation separated pathologic generalized slowing from clean-normal at **AUROC 0.784 in W (TAR) and 0.806 in N1 (log delta)** (N2 0.739, REM 0.771, N3 0.643; positives n = 2,294, held-out routine-normal negatives n = 1,839); a cross-validated sparse linear score reached **0.844** [0.827, 0.858]. We had previously reported 0.848/0.875 on the contaminated class, and we report the correction explicitly: **the inflated figures were an artifact of physiologic slowing scored as pathologic.** For the same reason we **withdraw the claim that normative references must be vigilance-matched**: on corrected labels the routine-versus-overnight reference gap collapses to ΔAUROC ≈ **0.003** (W) and **0.016** (N1), surviving only as a modest deep-sleep effect (N2 Δ0.022, N3 Δ0.047). The apparent "systematic degradation" was the label bug — drowsy physiologic slowing scored as pathologic is precisely what penalises a drowsy reference. **The foundation-model gate, not the normative deviation score, is the strongest detector, and it decisively outperforms the published qEEG benchmark.** Benchmarked head-to-head on 27,003 recordings against the van Putten family of slowing/asymmetry indices computed faithfully from the same signals, Morgoth `p_slowing` reached **AUROC 0.881 [0.876–0.885] (any slowing), 0.918 [0.913–0.923] (generalized) and 0.875 [0.870–0.881] (focal)**, versus the best published metric at **0.698 / 0.751 / 0.726** (r-sBSI, age-normed Q_SLOWING, r-sBSI respectively) — a margin of **+0.15 to +0.18 AUROC**. Age-conditioning his metrics on our normative curves improved them (e.g. Q_SLOWING 0.702 → 0.751 for generalized), confirming that lifespan normalization is a real gain — but it does not close the gap to the learned representation. The normative deviation field retains its role as the *description* layer: it is calibrated (clean-normals: prevalence 0.040 against a nominal 0.05; median amount −0.16 SD) and graded by the expert call (clean-normal −0.13 → focal +0.61 → generalized +0.99 SD). **We measured the human ceiling, and — unlike the report-label-based claims above — it replicates on the new run.** In an independent panel of 18 electroencephalographers reading 100 EEGs, slowing was the *least* reliable judgement made: between-rater Fleiss κ = 0.373 (focal) and 0.450 (generalized), versus 0.585 and 0.739 for epileptiform discharges; a reader re-reading the same EEG reproduced their own slowing call at only κ = 0.563 / 0.642. Applied unchanged to those 100 EEGs as an **external test set** — no refitting, no threshold tuning — our stage-matched deviation score reached **AUROC 0.903 [0.832, 0.958]** for generalized slowing against the expert majority, and after leave-one-out recalibration exceeded the average expert on balanced accuracy (0.835 vs 0.809), though *not* at the expert's own operating point (sensitivity 0.684 vs 0.735 at matched specificity). Our continuous score tracked the **proportion of experts who saw the slowing** at Spearman ρ = 0.652, versus ρ = 0.050 against the reader's severity adjective — the score is a measure of conspicuity, and it is the adjective, not the measurement, that is unreliable. **Band determination is near-chance** (κ 0.01–0.07 against per-expert band calls) and we do not claim it. Localization, evaluated as gated per-axis tasks rather than lumped labels, was strong for focal lateralization (left-vs-right AUROC 0.87, band-matched) and temporal-lobe localization (F1 0.64), modest for generalized anterior-vs-posterior predominance (AUROC 0.66), and data-limited for rarer posterior foci. Gate detection was age-dependent (abnormal-vs-normal AUROC 0.79 in children to 0.95 in older adults; report-flag AUCs abnormal 0.90, focal 0.79, generalized 0.75).

**Conclusions.** To our knowledge this is the first large-scale, lifespan- and sleep-stage-resolved, deviation-from-normal instrument for EEG slowing, whose outputs are expressed in — and validated against — the language of clinical EEG reports. We release an open, reproducible package and a per-recording label set.

## 1. Introduction

Slowing of the EEG background is the most common and most clinically consequential abnormality a neurophysiologist reports. Focal slowing points to a localized structural or functional lesion; generalized slowing signals diffuse encephalopathy. Yet whether a given rhythm counts as "slow" is inescapably relative: the posterior dominant rhythm that is normal at 4 Hz in an infant would be markedly abnormal in an adult, and delta activity that is pathological in the waking adult is entirely physiological in deep sleep. Interpretation is therefore age- and state-dependent, and in practice remains qualitative and expert-dependent — a source of inter-reader variability and a barrier to scalable, reproducible second reads.

**What is already known.** The direction of lifespan spectral change is textbook-settled: low-frequency (delta, theta) power dominates in infancy and early childhood and declines with maturation while faster rhythms increase, with the posterior dominant rhythm accelerating from ~3–4 Hz in infancy toward the adult 8–12 Hz alpha by adolescence; aperiodic (1/f) activity flattens with development. In aging the picture is subtler and health-dependent, and much apparent "aging slowing" reflects comorbidity rather than healthy aging. Modern quantification has taken two forms. First, EEG "brain age" regresses chronological age on resting spectral (and aperiodic) features, formalized as a reusable M/EEG benchmark by Engemann et al. (2022, *NeuroImage*). Second, and conceptually closest to us, normative centile modeling of brain measures: Bethlehem et al. (2022, *Nature*) built MRI "brain charts" across the lifespan (n = 101,457) using GAMLSS to yield individual deviation scores — the structural-imaging analog of what we do functionally with EEG. Crucially, almost all lifespan qEEG reports *normal values*, not the *deviation of an individual clinical EEG from its matched norm*, and essentially none is sleep-stage-specific.

**The historical substrate clinicians still use.** Day-to-day EEG reading rests on age norms established decades ago on modest samples. Petersén & Eeg-Olofsson (1971, *Neuropädiatrie*) characterized the developing EEG in children aged 1–15 years (a widely cited N ≈ 700+), qualitatively-to-semiquantitatively and awake-focused. The most direct precedent for our deviation framing is John et al. (1980, *Science* 210:1255), whose "developmental equations" gave 32 linear age regressions of band power, replicated across US and Swedish children; the companion paper (1980, *Science* 210:1258) explicitly used deviation from these equations to flag dysfunction — an early "deviation-from-normal" idea, but over a narrow age band, eyes-closed rest only, and a few hundred subjects. John et al. (1988, *Science* 239:162), "Neurometrics," and the commercial normative databases it seeded (NeuroGuide/NxLink lineage) generalized age-regressed z-scoring, but these remain wake-resting, age-banded, of modest and somewhat opaque N, and not reproducible.

**Disease-specific abnormality indices.** A separate literature quantifies pathology directly without lifespan normalization. van Putten & Tavy (2004, *Stroke* 35:2489) introduced the Brain Symmetry Index (BSI), a 0–1 measure of interhemispheric spectral asymmetry correlating strongly with stroke severity (n = 21); the revised pairwise BSI followed (van Putten 2007, *Clin Neurophysiol* 118:2362). This is the intellectual ancestor of our homologous-channel asymmetry feature. Slowing ratios such as DAR and (delta+theta)/(alpha+beta) track acute-stroke severity (Finnigan & van Putten 2013), and relative delta/theta and alpha-delta ratios discriminate ICU delirium and coma. Every such instrument is tied to one disease and setting, uses fixed thresholds, and ignores age, sex, and — universally — sleep stage.

**Automated interpretation.** The Temple University Hospital Abnormal corpus (TUAB; López et al. 2015/16; Obeid & Picone 2016) established binary normal/abnormal classification as the standard benchmark, with ConvNets reaching ~85% (Schirrmeister et al. 2017; Gemein et al. 2020). EEG foundation models (BENDR, LaBraM, and clinically grounded variants) now dominate representation learning; our Morgoth heads sit in this family but are used as a calibrated *gate* rather than an end-to-end black box. Report NLP has extracted findings from free text (Biswal et al. 2015, *IEEE EMBC*, senior author Westover), and recent systems generate narrative from signal — but none ties generated findings to a lifespan-normative deviation model, nor validates stage-specific slowing sentences against the actual report corpus.

**The gap.** No prior work combines (a) lifespan-continuous, (b) sex-specific, and (c) sleep-stage-specific normative modeling of clinical slowing features with (d) per-recording deviation-from-normal scoring, (e) at the scale of ~12,000 patients from routine practice, and (f) closes the loop to clinician-style narrative validated against real reports. A full comparison against prior work is given in **Table T2** (from the literature review). (Caveat: exact Ns and page-level attributions for the historical norms should be re-verified before submission.)

**Contributions.** This paper delivers: (1) reproducible age × sex × sleep-stage normative growth curves for clinical slowing features across the lifespan; (2) a deviation-from-normal scoring framework (z, burden, prevalence, persistence, stage-accentuation, homologous asymmetry); (3) a Morgoth-gated focal/generalized decision coupling a self-supervised foundation model to interpretable norms; (4) automatic generation of clinician-style report sentences validated against free-text reports; and (5) an open Python package with a published per-recording label set.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Cohort and data

The study uses the *Growth_curves* dataset of routine clinical EEGs curated from a single academic health system (MGB sites), comprising 12,379 recordings from approximately 12,000 patients. Each recording carries report-derived structured finding flags (normal, abnormal, focal slowing, generalized slowing), which are **non-exclusive** — a report may note more than one. A key correction in this revision concerns the *normal reference*: because a study called normal cannot, by definition, harbor pathological slowing, we define controls as **clean normal** — flagged normal and *not* abnormal/focal/generalized — yielding n ≈ 2,501, rather than the naive "any-normal-flag" group (n ≈ 5,877) that a prior priority-cascade produced and which we found was **~57% contaminated** with recordings also carrying a slowing flag (their inclusion biased the normative curves toward more slowing). Slowing groups (focal n ≈ 2,742; generalized n ≈ 5,720, with substantial overlap) are evaluated **one-vs-clean-normal** rather than as a forced multiclass. We also corrected two label-extraction defects found during analysis (§2.8): a structured-flag under-count (findings encoded "verified"/"annotation" rather than "report" were dropped, ~8% of focal / ~5% of generalized) and a broken report-text side/region extractor. Labels ultimately derive from the interpreting clinician's report. *(Counts are being finalized on the full-cohort re-run.)* Cohort demographics are summarized in **Table T1**. The median age across all recordings is 53.0 years (Q1–Q3 26.0–69.0). Sex was resolved from OMOP (`gender_concept_id`) for 12,378 of 12,379 recordings and is balanced at 49.5% female / 50.5% male overall. Eighteen recordings with implausible ages (<0 or >120 years) were excluded. **Patients contributing more than one recording were also excluded** (350 patients, 702 recordings, 5.67% of the cohort): the recording identifier available to us (`bdsp_id`) resolves a patient at a site rather than an individual study, and the segment-level feature tables are keyed on it, so a patient with two studies would have one study's features paired with the other's labels. Excluding them removes that ambiguity at the cost of 5.67% of recordings; the excluded patients were, if anything, *less* likely to be abnormal than those retained (33.1% vs 48.8%), so the exclusion does not enrich the cohort for pathology. Importantly, controls skew roughly 20 years younger than the slowing groups (control median 38.0 years versus focal 61.0 and generalized 58.0), and 75+ controls are relatively sparse (n = 442) — an age confound that motivates conditioning all scoring on age and sex. Age is taken from the recording datetime encoded in each filename (`sub-<BDSP_ID>_<YYYYMMDDHHMMSS>`) combined with the OMOP birth date; sex from OMOP. Reproducible cohort metadata (`metadata/cohort_metadata.csv`) regenerates Table T1 and the curves without S3 access.

### 2.2 Reproducible feature extraction

The original *Growth_curves* features were precomputed in MATLAB by a collaborator, but that code is unavailable. To make the pipeline reproducible and extensible to new recordings, we reimplemented extraction in Python (`features/extract.py`; `scripts/13_recompute_features.py` regenerated all 12,379 recordings). The pipeline re-montages the referential 10-20 EEG to 18 bipolar (double-banana) channels, applies a 0.5 Hz high-pass and 50/60 Hz notch, segments into 15-second windows (3000 samples at 200 Hz, step 2800), and computes a multitaper power spectral density (time-bandwidth NW = 4, 7 tapers). Per segment we derive 31 features — delta/theta/alpha/beta/gamma power, total power, relative powers, and inter-band ratios (including DAR = delta/alpha and TAR = theta/alpha) — for each of the 18 bipolar channels, plus 8 homologous left–right pairs for focal localization. Channels are aggregated into regions (whole-head, left/right temporal, left/right parasagittal, midline) via `config/channels_regions.yaml`. Linear powers are log-transformed before z-scoring.

**Validation.** We validated the Python features against the original precomputed set (`scripts/12_validate_extractor.py`): per-band log-power correlations were r = 0.89–0.95 (delta 0.89, theta 0.90, alpha 0.89, beta 0.95, total 0.87; gamma 0.79, which is filtering-sensitive). Downstream science was preserved: stage physiology (normal median relative delta N3 > N2 > W/REM > N1) and discrimination both reproduced, and the newly added per-channel homologous asymmetry became a top focal discriminator (see §3.4).

**Calibration.** Because relative power depends on the low-frequency edge and total-band definition, uncalibrated relative delta ran high (normal whole-head median ~0.5–0.6 versus a physiological reference near 0.30). We calibrated the delta band to 1–4 Hz, bringing normal whole-head relative delta to 0.34 (physiological). The full pipeline was recomputed after calibration and all validation metrics held (report agreement region 0.92 / side 0.79 / band 0.74; LR↔Morgoth r 0.64–0.69; discrimination unchanged). Because all deviation scoring is computed as z relative to each feature's own age/sex/stage normal curve, the scoring, discrimination, and generated descriptions are scale-invariant and were unaffected by this calibration; only raw relative-power magnitudes changed.

**Artifact rejection.** Norms and reports must reflect clean brain signal, not flat or contaminated segments. A per-segment artifact filter (`features/artifact.py`) marks a 15-second bipolar segment unusable if it is flat/disconnected (median channel peak-to-peak < 1 µV, or >50% of channels flat), high-amplitude (any channel peak-to-peak > 500 µV, e.g. movement or electrode pop), or EMG-dominated (fraction of 1–45 Hz power above 20 Hz > 0.55); the usable-segment fraction is carried into scoring so low-yield recordings are flagged rather than silently scored on a handful of segments. On a real full-length BIDS EDF that was largely disconnected, rejection kept 93/253 segments (37%) and recovered a physiological whole-head relative delta of 0.32 from an artifact-inflated 0.047 (`docs/artifact_rejection_plan.md`). Thresholds are provisional pending manual-review calibration.

**Full-recording ingestion.** The same extractor runs unchanged on full multi-hour recordings through a validated ingestion path (`io/edf.py`) that harmonizes heterogeneous BIDS EDFs (variable sampling rate, 30–50 channels, old/new 10-20 nomenclature) to the 19 referential channels at 200 Hz, then applies artifact rejection, whole-recording staging, and per-stage features. This pilot ran end-to-end with cohort-range relative delta and realistic W/N1/N2/N3/REM staging, establishing the route to lifespan-scale expansion — in particular the overnight/deep-sleep recordings needed to fill the adult N3 gap (a cloud-scale run is planned; `docs/aws_cloud_plan.md`).

### 2.3 Sleep staging

Routine EEG feature sets are unstaged (all segments marked "Other"). We staged each recording from the raw EEG with the morgoth2 deep-learning stager (window-level 5-class model `ss_hm_1.pth`; the architecturally identical `SLEEPPSG.pth` serves as substitute), run on Apple MPS. The model reads continuous raw segments (200 Hz) and emits per-second, 5-class (W/N1/N2/N3/REM) predictions at a 1-second step; we assign each 15-second feature segment its majority stage. Stage distributions were validated against expectations for routine clips (predominantly wake with drowsy/N1/N2) and cross-checked against a labeled sleep-staging set. The one operational constraint was Python-environment isolation (the stager's dependency `pyhealth` pins pandas <2, incompatible with the analysis stack), resolved with a dedicated staging virtual environment.

### 2.4 Normative growth curves (GAMLSS/LMS)

For each feature × region × sleep stage we estimated normal-population percentile "growth curves" as a continuous function of age using **GAMLSS/LMS** — the method behind clinical growth charts (Cole & Green 1992; Rigby & Stasinopoulos 2005). We model a Box–Cox-t (BCT) distribution with penalized-spline median, dispersion, **and age-varying skewness** (`mu`, `sigma`, `nu` each smooth in log-age); allowing skewness to vary with age removes a systematic infant-age median bias that a constant-skewness fit produces. Age enters as `log10(age + 1/12)`, expanding infancy where maturation is fastest, on precise OMOP-derived fractional ages. Each curve is validated against a **model-free rolling median in a sliding, age-widening window**, which the LMS median tracks closely, and by band coverage (≈50% within p25–p75, ≈95% within p3–p97).

**Sex is pooled.** Conditioning norms on sex changes abnormality discrimination by ΔAUROC ≤ 0.002 in every feature × region × contrast we tested, and the median sex effect is 0.01–0.02; we therefore pool sexes, doubling the effective sample per age.

**The normal reference is the union of both report-normal cohorts** (routine + overnight). Both are clinician-read normal, so the union captures the true clinical range of "normal," which is broader than either cohort alone — a deliberately conservative choice. We verified it costs nothing: substituting the union reference for a single-cohort reference leaves abnormal-vs-normal AUROC statistically unchanged.

### 2.5 Vigilance-matched norms, and why the two cohorts differ

Routine and overnight recordings differ systematically, and we established *why* before pooling. (i) **Channels are identical** in both pipelines. (ii) **A same-recording pipeline control** — recomputing routine EEGs with the shared extractor and comparing to the legacy features on the identical recordings — gave a rel_delta bias of −0.016, so the feature computation is consistent. (iii) The residual difference is therefore **physiological**: the two cohorts are 100% disjoint patient populations (outpatient vs inpatient), and, decisively, **overnight "wake" is not the same state as routine wake** (relative alpha 0.06 vs 0.24; higher delta) — because a routine EEG is recorded under *active alerting* by the technologist, whereas overnight wake is unconstrained and frequently drowsy. Adult N2/N3 agree across cohorts, as a pipeline artifact would not permit.

This has a direct methodological consequence: **detection requires a vigilance-matched reference.** Scoring an alert routine EEG against a drowsy overnight norm inflates the normal band and masks pathological slowing. We therefore use the union for the descriptive growth charts, but a **routine (alert) reference in W/N1** for detection, and we report the dependence explicitly (§3.4). To eliminate any residual pipeline confound, **all ~27,000 recordings were reprocessed through the single identical extractor and stager.**

### 2.6 Report–recording pairing and label provenance

Reports reach us in a table that was joined to EEGs **at the patient level** upstream: each EEG row carries at most one report identifier, but a single report is stamped onto a mean of 2.9 EEGs of that patient (maximum 170), and 53.4% of (patient, date) rows hold report text byte-identical to another row for the same patient. Joining a report to a recording on patient identity alone — or even on (patient, recording date) — therefore selects a row whose free text may describe a *different* study of that patient. We detected this because the reader's own severity adjective appeared to agree across a patient's consecutive studies 97% of the time (ρ = 0.95, n = 5,226 pairs), a reliability no clinical rating attains.

We assign each report to the EEG nearest it in time, and define a recording as **cleanly paired** when its report is claimed by no other EEG, or when it is that report's nearest-in-time owner. **82.8%** of recordings (10,255/12,379) are cleanly paired; 17.2% carry a borrowed report or none. All report-text-derived quantities (severity and frequency adjectives) are computed on cleanly-paired recordings only. Because the timing field is an order-to-study offset (median |Δ| = 14.2 h for uniquely-owned reports), this rule is a well-founded heuristic rather than a guarantee, and we flag it as a limitation (§5).

Diagnosis flags (normal / abnormal / focal slowing / generalized slowing) come from a separate per-recording findings table and are substantially recording-specific (only 34.5% of multi-study patients share an identical flag vector across studies). Our labels combine that flag with a text-derived term, so contamination can leak in; we therefore re-ran the primary detection analysis on cleanly-paired recordings alone, and every AUROC moved within its bootstrap confidence interval (**[LEGACY figures, pre-label-correction:** W 0.848→0.847; N1 0.875→0.872; N2 0.791→0.787; N3 0.758→0.749; REM 0.825→0.830; `results/detection_pairing_sensitivity.md`**; this sensitivity analysis is pending re-run on the corrected labels]**). This asymmetry is expected: a borrowed report belongs to the same patient, and abnormal-versus-normal status is stable across a patient's studies, whereas severity is not — which is precisely why detection is robust and severity grading (§3.4b) is not. Note that report *pairing* was never the dominant label problem: the far larger error was the physiologic-versus-pathologic confusion in the text extractor itself (§3.1), which no pairing filter would have caught.

### 2.7 Deviation scoring

Each recording is scored as deviation from its matched norm. For every segment we compute a z-score against the age/sex/stage normal curve, then summarize at the recording level: **prevalence** (fraction of usable segments abnormal, mapped to ACNS-style frequency words rare→continuous), **conditional severity** (magnitude when present, mapped to mild/moderate/marked), **burden** (combining prevalence and severity), **persistence** (longest continuous run, number of episodes, median episode duration), **stage-accentuation** (the stage with maximum per-stage burden; whether present only in sleep), and **homologous asymmetry** (left–right z on homologous pairs, for lateralization). A patient-level empirical-percentile z summarizes overall deviation. Because every segment is scored against *its own stage's* normal curve, delta that is abnormal in wake but normal in N2/N3 yields near-zero prevalence in normals.

### 2.7b The measurement and the score: two objects, and a rule

Two quantities are used throughout, and conflating them would make the paper circular.

**z, the measurement.** For every recording, region and sleep stage, we compute the deviation of each spectral feature from the age-matched clinician-normal distribution (Gaussian age kernel, bandwidth 5 y, reference = routine-alert clean-normals). `z` is **unsupervised**: it is fit to nothing but the normal population, and its value does not depend on whether any reader noticed the abnormality. All descriptive statements — regional deviation, stage-accentuation, the sleep analyses of §3.4e — are statements about z.

**S, the score.** For detection we fit an **L1-regularised logistic model on the z's**, retain only the features it selects, and report the **linear predictor** — the weighted sum of z-scores — rather than the fitted probability. The probability saturates near 0 and 1 and destroys grading (our earlier logistic scores were markedly over-confident, §3.5); the logit is unbounded, linear in the z's, and monotone in evidence. Fitting on z's rather than raw powers means each coefficient is interpretable in standard deviations of the normal population.

Because our features are strongly collinear (relative delta, absolute delta, DAR and TAR all track slowing), raw importances split credit arbitrarily. We therefore collapse correlation clusters (|r| ≥ 0.80) to one representative each, choose the penalty by the **one-standard-error rule** (the most-regularised model within one standard error of the best), and assess feature stability by bootstrap re-selection. **All of it — the normal reference, the clusters, the penalty and the selection — is re-derived inside each outer cross-validation fold**, split on patient, so nothing about a test fold informs its own score.

**Negative sets are part of the design.** For generalized slowing the comparison group is clean-normals. For focal slowing it is clean-normals **plus** recordings with pathologic generalized slowing, because 60.9% of focal recordings also carry generalized slowing and a detector trained against healthy controls alone would be rewarded for finding any slowing at all rather than focality. We report the resulting score against three comparison groups (§3.4c-bis), since each answers a different clinical question.

**The rule.** `S` is trained to predict the expert's call and therefore inherits the expert's blind spots by construction. **`S` is never used to argue that we detect slowing readers miss.** That argument rests on `z` alone (§3.4e). Conversely, `z` is never tuned to any label.

**What we do not model: the band.** Experts agree with each other on the band of slowing at κ = 0.09–0.38 (§3.6). An L1 model fit to a target carrying that little reliable signal selects noise. We report the ceiling and decline the axis.

### 2.8 Topography and the Morgoth gate

Detection and description are deliberately separated (`docs/report_architecture.md`): the Morgoth foundation-model heads, calibrated against expert reports, decide *whether* and *what* to report; our normative features decide *how to describe* it. The gate is three-tier and hierarchical. **Tier 1** (`NORMAL.pth` + EEG-level aggregator) asks whether the EEG is abnormal at all; if normal, the pipeline says so and stops. **Tier 2** (`SLOWING.pth`) asks whether pathological slowing specifically is present, with high specificity to expert reporting so slowing is not over-called on normative deviation alone; only this tier gates our slowing description. **Tier 3** (`FOC_SLOWING_EEGlevel.pth`, `GEN_SLOWING_EEGlevel.pth`) sets the topographic branch(es); focal and generalized are separate, non-mutually-exclusive heads, so multifocal/mixed cases can fire both. Each head is two-stage: a window-level model reading raw data, then an EEG-level aggregator producing one probability per recording (`scripts/14_aggregate_gate.py`; outputs described in `docs/morgoth_gate_outputs.md`). Consistency between the gate and our features is itself a signal: when Morgoth calls focal but asymmetry z ≈ 0, confidence is lowered and morphology review is recommended.

### 2.9 Report generation

Once a branch is gated in, a sentence is templated from the kept features (`scripts/16_gated_report.py`): location (region + side from regional burden and asymmetry z), band/type (delta vs theta vs mixed; pathological slowing usually defaults to mixed unless one band clearly dominates), severity (patient-level z), prevalence/intermittency (ACNS words), persistence (longest run, episodes), and state-dependence (present in wake vs only in sleep; the most-accentuated stage). For generalized slowing, low relative alpha/theta adds "paucity of faster activity."

### 2.10 Validation and statistics

We validated at three levels. **Against Morgoth** (`scripts/17`): agreement of our deviation-based logistic-regression probability with Morgoth probabilities (Pearson/Spearman) and distillation R² of our features onto Morgoth probabilities. **Against report flags** (`scripts/19`): AUC of Morgoth and of our scores against report-derived abnormal/focal/generalized flags. **Against report text** (`scripts/20`): agreement of our generated statement with the report where the report states region, side, or band. Discrimination (`scripts/06`) reports age- and sex-adjusted AUCs of each feature's normal-referenced z between groups; feature selection (`scripts/15`) combines L1-logistic (LASSO), random-forest importance, and bootstrap stability selection with correlation clustering to yield a parsimonious, de-duplicated keep-list, distilling both the clinical label and Morgoth's abnormality call.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Cohort and coverage

The final cohort comprises **27,012 recordings** — **10,966 routine** and **16,046 overnight** studies — spanning infancy to >90 years, all processed through one identical pipeline (**Table 1**; `results/table1.md`). The normative reference is the union of both report-normal groups, **n = 20,892 clean-normal** (4,846 routine + 16,046 overnight); abnormal recordings number **5,557**, of which 3,942 carry pathologic generalized slowing and 3,727 focal slowing. Sex is balanced throughout (≈50% female in every group). **Age is a genuine confound and motivates the entire normative design**: abnormal recordings are markedly older than normals (median 60.0 y [IQR 35.9–72.5] vs 47.0 y [21.5–65.7]), and routine clean-normals younger still (37.5 y), so any unadjusted comparison would substantially conflate slowing with age. The overnight studies supply the deep-sleep coverage routine clips cannot: N2/N3/REM each now rest on ~15,000 recordings across the lifespan, closing the adult-N3 gap that truncated earlier curves. Legacy coverage notes below refer to the pre-expansion cohort (**Table T1**). All nine age bands are represented, with the largest counts at 60–74 (n = 3,027) and 45–59 (n = 2,329) and the smallest in early childhood (3–5: n = 321). Sex is balanced within every group. The control group is younger than the slowing groups, reinforcing the need for age/sex conditioning. Coverage by sleep stage × age (`docs/coverage_by_stage.md`) shows that wake, N1, and N2 are well powered across the whole lifespan in normals (mostly hundreds per age band, minimum 73), so curves for these stages are trustworthy including the tails. N3 is under-powered from age ≥3 (154 recordings at 0–2 years, then 13–42 per band) — partly physiological (N3 declines with age) and partly a sampling limitation of short routine clips — so adult N3 curves have wide confidence intervals and are truncated by the min-n guard. REM is adequate in adults but thin at the extremes (62 at 0–2; 69 at 75+). Abnormal cases are too sparse per stage × age cell for stage-specific validation (often 0–5 per cell), so stage-specific discrimination is treated as exploratory and validation is performed stage-pooled on the well-powered groups (2,067 focal / 5,396 generalized).

### 3.2 Growth curves reproduce development and aging

The normative curves recapitulate textbook maturation. Absolute delta power falls steeply through childhood to a plateau by approximately 30 years (**Figure F1**; `figures/curves/log_delta__whole_head.png`), consistent with the well-established slow-to-fast developmental shift. Normal-referenced z-scores center on 0 for controls across the lifespan, confirming that the curves are well-calibrated to the normal population. Curves for all 8 features × 5 regions are provided in `figures/curves/`.

### 3.3 Sleep-stage-specific norms

Stage-resolved curves quantify the expected physiology of slowing across sleep depth. In normals, median relative delta rises with sleep depth: **W ≈ N1 < N2 < N3**, with REM intermediate (**Figure F2**; `figures/stage_curves/rel_delta__whole_head.png`). The direct clinical consequence is that delta which is abnormal in wake is entirely normal in N2/N3; because each segment is scored against its own stage's normal curve, normal prevalence approaches zero even in sleep-heavy recordings. Regional stage-specific curves (`figures/regional_stage_curves/`) extend this per region.

An initial obstacle was that the original cohort's sleep staging had been run only on the normative (normal) recordings, leaving the abnormal recordings unstaged — so a stage-stratified *abnormal-versus-normal* analysis was not possible. We corrected this by staging the abnormal recordings directly from their archived raw clips (`scripts/36`), yielding staged focal and generalized cases. A stage-stratified detection analysis (whole-head summed deviation of rel_delta/DAR/TAR from age-matched normals, abnormal vs normal AUROC per age band; **Figure F2b**, `results/age_auroc_by_stage.csv`) then shows that this simple spectral score is **modest and age-dependent in every stage** (AUROC ≈ 0.44 in youth rising to ≈ 0.70 in older adults, similar across W/N2/N3/REM). Notably the whole-head average dilutes focal (localized) slowing, so recording-level detection with the full feature set is far stronger than this per-stage whole-head score — the stage-resolved curves are most useful for *normalizing physiology*, not as a stand-alone detector.

### 3.4 Detection is vigilance-dependent, and the norm must match

On the completed clean-room run (v6: 27,478 recordings, one extractor, one stager, zero reuse of prior
tables) and the **corrected** slowing labels, age-adjusted whole-head deviation separated **pathologic
generalized slowing** from clean-normal at **AUROC 0.784 in W (TAR)** and **0.806 in N1 (log delta)** —
N1 remaining the single most discriminating stage — with 0.771 in REM, 0.739 in N2 and 0.643 in N3
(**Figure 2**; `results/vigilance_matched_detection.csv`; positives n = 2,294, held-out routine-normal
negatives n = 1,839). A cross-validated sparse linear score reached **0.844** [0.827, 0.858]
(`results/sparse_slowing_score.md`).

**We correct two claims from earlier drafts of this work.** First, the previously reported 0.848 (W) /
0.875 (N1) were obtained on a **contaminated positive class**. The conventional report extractor matched
slowing terms in the impression *concatenated with the whole report body*, so descriptive statements such
as *"generalized slowing, likely related to intermittent drowsiness"* — physiologic slowing in a study the
reader called normal — entered the pathologic class. Restricting the class to slowing that the report
**names among its abnormalities** (§2.x; `label_rederive_sap.py`) reclassified **5,528 recordings** and
moved the positive count from 3,883 to 2,294. The corrected figures above are lower and are the ones we
stand behind. An ablation isolates the term: on the identical cell (W, whole-head, TAR), contaminated
labels give 0.655 and corrected labels 0.766 with artifact segments dropped, against 0.649/0.620 when
artifact segments are retained (`results/ablation_auroc.md`).

Second — and following directly from the first — **we withdraw the claim that normative references must be
vigilance-matched.** That claim rested on a large routine-versus-overnight reference gap (W 0.848 vs 0.821;
N1 0.875 vs 0.791). On corrected labels the gap essentially disappears in the alert stages: **W 0.784
(routine) vs 0.781 (overnight) vs 0.783 (union), ΔAUROC ≈ 0.003; N1 0.806 vs 0.790 vs 0.805, Δ ≈ 0.016.**
A modest effect persists only in deeper sleep (N2 Δ 0.022; N3 Δ 0.047), where a drowsy reference plausibly
does absorb genuine pathology. In hindsight the original result is easy to read: physiologic *drowsy*
slowing miscounted as pathologic is exactly the error that penalises a *drowsy* reference, manufacturing an
apparent vigilance effect. The honest statement is that the choice of normal reference is **second-order**
next to the correctness of the labels, and that age-conditioning — not vigilance-conditioning — carries the
normative benefit (§3.1b).

### 3.1b The Morgoth gate decisively outperforms the published qEEG benchmark

The strongest detector in this study is not the normative deviation score but the foundation-model gate, and
the margin over the established quantitative-EEG literature is large. We benchmarked against the van Putten
family of indices — the Brain Symmetry Index and its revised pairwise form, the diffuse-slowing index
Q_SLOWING (reported at κ = 0.76 against expert readers), the anterior–posterior gradient Q_APG, the
homologous-pair asymmetry Q_ASYM, and the slowing ratios DAR and (δ+θ)/(α+β) — computing each **faithfully
from the same signals and the same artifact-flagged segments**, rather than re-deriving approximations
(`scripts/producer_vanputten_sap.py`, `scripts/recompute_vanputten_fullcov.py`). Each was evaluated in three
arms per the analysis plan: raw as published, age-conditioned against our normative curves, and against our
gate. On **27,003 recordings** (`results/vanputten_fullcoverage.md`; **Table 6**):

| Method | any slowing | generalized | focal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Q_SLOWING (raw; van Putten 2013) | 0.654 | 0.702 | 0.630 |
| r-sBSI (raw; van Putten 2007) | 0.698 | 0.692 | 0.726 |
| Q_ASYM (raw) | 0.684 | 0.690 | 0.697 |
| Q_SLOWING (age-normed, ours) | 0.692 | **0.751** | 0.671 |
| **Morgoth p_slowing (gate)** | **0.881** [0.876–0.885] | **0.918** [0.913–0.923] | **0.875** [0.870–0.881] |

Two results follow. First, **lifespan normalization is a genuine gain even for existing instruments**:
age-conditioning Q_SLOWING against our curves raises generalized detection from 0.702 to 0.751, and the
same is true across the family — evidence that the normative framing, not merely a new classifier, adds
information to metrics that have been used unnormalized for two decades. Second, **it does not close the
gap**: the learned representation exceeds the best published metric by **+0.18 (any slowing), +0.17
(generalized) and +0.15 (focal) AUROC**, far outside the bootstrap intervals. This is the central
quantitative claim of the paper, and it is what motivates the two-stage architecture: the gate decides
*whether* slowing is present, and the normative field — which is calibrated and graded by the expert call,
but is a weaker detector — supplies the *description*.

### 3.4b Dose-response across report strata — and the failure to reproduce the severity *grade*

Deviation is not merely correlated with the expert call; it is graded by it (**Figure 3**). Scored in N1 against the routine reference, the median age-adjusted z rises monotonically across report strata — **clean-normal ≈ 0** (−0.11 log-delta, −0.00 TAR, −0.05 DAR: the reference is well calibrated), **abnormal with no slowing named +0.33 to +0.43**, and **abnormal with slowing named +1.33 to +1.49** (Spearman ρ = 0.50–0.55, p ≈ 0; Kruskal–Wallis p ≈ 0; n = 4,451 / 722 / 4,363). Two things follow. First, the score is anchored at the normal population rather than being an arbitrary detector output. Second, the **middle stratum is informative**: recordings the reader called abnormal *without naming slowing* already deviate by ≈ +0.4 SD, consistent with slowing that was present but not stated — the quantitative signature underlying the "detects what reports under-report" hypothesis (§4).

**This does not extend to the reader's severity adjective, and we report that as a negative result.** Among recordings whose report names slowing, our score does *not* recover the mild/moderate/marked grade: Spearman **ρ = 0.050 (p = 0.17, n = 753)**, scored in W/N1, in the max-deviation region for focal cases, against the vigilance-matched reference, and restricted to recordings that own the report written about them (§2.6). This survives an exhaustive search: across **168 combinations** of 7 features × 4 summary statistics × {raw, age-normalized} × {generalized, focal, all}, the largest |ρ| anywhere is **0.179**, which does not survive Bonferroni correction (0.05/168 = 3.0 × 10⁻⁴; best p = 6.8 × 10⁻⁴) and carries the *wrong sign* (`results/severity_axis_sweep.md`). Raw and age-normalized scores perform equivalently (0.159 vs 0.179), so this is not an artifact of normalization. Reported **prevalence** fares only marginally better against the reader's frequency word (ρ = 0.077, p = 3.3 × 10⁻⁶, n = 3,626): statistically significant, clinically negligible.

We therefore claim detection and dose-response, **not** severity grading. The most likely explanation is that the adjective carries little reliable quantitative information: in an independent panel of 18 electroencephalographers reading 100 EEGs (§3.x), slowing was the *least* reliable judgement made — between-rater Fleiss κ = 0.373 (focal) and 0.450 (generalized), versus 0.585 and 0.739 for epileptiform discharges — and a reader re-reading the *same* EEG on a second occasion reproduced their own slowing call at only κ = 0.563 / 0.642. Band determination was less reliable still (pairwise κ 0.09–0.38). An adjective attached to a judgement of that reliability cannot support a strong quantitative correlation with any measurement.

### 3.4c Which features discriminate

Age- and sex-adjusted AUCs (`results/discrimination.md`; **Table T3**, **Figure F3**) identify a small, clinically interpretable discriminative set. For **normal vs focal**, TAR led (L_temporal AUC 0.704, whole-head 0.697), followed closely by the per-channel homologous asymmetry `|asym_ch_T3–T5_delta|` (AUC 0.696) and temporal delta asymmetry (0.682); theta and delta power followed (log_theta whole-head 0.681; log_delta whole-head 0.658). The strong showing of homologous asymmetry validates the per-channel/homologous-pair features as focal discriminators. For **normal vs generalized**, TAR again led (whole-head 0.727; R_parasagittal 0.725; L_temporal 0.725), with DAR next (L_temporal 0.685) and delta power behind (whole-head 0.659). Distinguishing **focal from generalized** by spectral deviation alone was weak (best AUCs ~0.55–0.58, e.g., DAR R_parasagittal 0.576), consistent with topography rather than spectral shape being the discriminating axis — which is exactly what the Morgoth gate supplies. Age/sex adjustment mattered: adjusted AUCs consistently exceeded age-confounded raw AUCs (e.g., log_theta whole-head 0.681 adjusted vs 0.584 raw).

Feature selection (`results/feature_selection.md`) combining L1-logistic, random-forest importance, and bootstrap stability under correlation clustering converged on a compact keep-list — **TAR (θ/α), DAR (δ/α), log-δ, and log-θ** (whole-head plus temporal/parasagittal), all with stability 1.0 — plus per-channel homologous asymmetry (e.g., T3–T5 delta) for focal localization. The same features rose to the top whether the target was the expert label or a distillation of Morgoth's abnormality call (e.g., TAR whole-head RF importance 0.111 for the Morgoth-abnormal target), indicating that our interpretable features reconstruct much of the expert-matched detector.

### 3.4c-bis A sparse score: three features for generalized slowing, eight for focal

Fitting the L1 model on the normative deviations and reading off the retained features (**Figure 3b**; `results/sparse_slowing_score.md`), **generalized slowing needs three**: TAR at the midline in wake (β = +0.51), absolute delta over the whole head in N1 (+0.44), and DAR over the whole head in N1 (+0.05). All coefficients are positive; none is a collinearity suppressor. The nested cross-validated AUROC of that three-feature linear predictor is **0.908**, against **0.934** for the dense 28-feature model — parsimony costs 0.025, and the curve is flat beyond ten features. Applied with coefficients frozen in-cohort to the 100 expert-read EEGs, it reaches **AUROC 0.909 [0.840, 0.962]** against the expert majority — marginally above both the Morgoth gate (0.895) and a hand-tuned score (0.903) — and tracks the consensus proportion at Spearman **ρ = 0.661** (`results/sparse_score_external.md`).

**The focal detector is trained against normals *and* generalized slowing.** This is forced by the labels: a report naming focal slowing does not exclude generalized slowing, and in fact **60.9% of focal recordings also carry pathologic generalized slowing**. A detector trained against healthy controls alone would therefore be rewarded for finding *any* slowing. Including generalized recordings among the negatives removes that route, and the L1 path responds by retaining **only asymmetry and lobar terms** — temporal delta asymmetry in N1 first (β = +0.17), then temporal DAR asymmetry, left-temporal delta, and parasagittal asymmetries. No whole-head or midline term survives.

That single score answers three different questions, and the answers differ (**Table 2**):

| positives | comparison group | nested AUROC | external, expert majority |
|---|---|---|---|
| all focal | clean-normal | **0.857** | 0.855 [0.729, 0.957] |
| all focal | clean-normal + generalized | **0.798** | 0.848 [0.731, 0.946] |
| all focal | generalized | **0.622** | 0.746 [0.554, 0.909] |
| exclusively focal | clean-normal | 0.759 | 0.848 [0.718, 0.956] |
| exclusively focal | clean-normal + generalized | 0.688 | 0.841 [0.717, 0.953] |
| **exclusively focal** | **generalized** | **0.477** | 0.739 [0.541, 0.905] |

Read down the last two rows. Against clean-normals, and against the mixed comparison group that a deployed system actually faces, the score performs well. But asked to separate **exclusively focal** recordings from generalized ones, it is at chance in-cohort (0.477) and, on the smaller expert panel, carries a confidence interval spanning 0.54 to 0.91. The apparent 0.622 for "all focal versus generalized" is an artifact of the 60.9% of focal recordings that *also* have generalized slowing: the score is partly detecting their generalized component.

**We therefore claim focal detection, not focal-versus-generalized discrimination.** This is consistent with the age/sex-adjusted single-feature analysis (§3.4c: focal-versus-generalized AUC 0.55–0.58) and with the regional deviation pattern (§3.6: focal slowing raises the contralateral hemisphere by +0.5 to +0.8 SD, and the parasagittal chain lateralizes almost as well as the temporal chain — the signal is hemispheric, not lobar). Distinguishing focal from generalized is a topographic judgement, and it is what the Morgoth gate supplies.

Agreement with a single clinical report is bounded by the reliability of that report, which the paper had never measured. We therefore scored an independent, multiply-read dataset: **100 EEGs, 18 electroencephalographers**, each recording judged for focal and generalized epileptiform and non-epileptiform abnormality, with a subset re-read on a second occasion (`results/occasion_human_ceiling.md`).

**Slowing is the least reliable judgement experts make.** Between-rater Fleiss κ was **0.373** for focal slowing and **0.450** for generalized slowing, against **0.585** and **0.739** for focal and generalized epileptiform discharges. Fifteen experts who re-read the same EEGs reproduced *their own* slowing call at κ = **0.563** (focal) and **0.642** (generalized) — versus 0.716 and 0.832 for discharges. Band was less reliable still: conditional on two experts both calling slowing, they chose the same band on only 54.1% (focal) and 26.6% (generalized) of events (`results/moe_human_ceiling.md`). On EEGs whose *signed clinical report* called them focal non-epileptiform, only **50.8%** of the panel marked focal slowing. Every "agreement with the report" figure in this literature, including ours, is bounded by these numbers.

**The system is two-stage, and is evaluated as such (Figure 4).** Morgoth decides *whether and what*; the normative deviations say *how much*. Against the expert majority on these 100 EEGs the gate reaches **AUROC 0.900 [0.822, 0.963]** for generalized slowing and **0.923 [0.817, 0.995]** for focal. Our linear predictors then behave as quantifiers should: S(generalized) is **+0.77** in generalized-slowing EEGs versus **−0.38** in those the panel calls neither (p = 3.3 × 10⁻⁸), and is *not* elevated in focal-only EEGs (p = 0.24); S(focal) is **+0.23** versus **−0.14** (p = 3.0 × 10⁻⁵). S(focal) also rises modestly in generalized-only EEGs (p = 5.7 × 10⁻³) — the focal-versus-generalized ambiguity of §3.4c-bis — which is precisely what gating on the model's focal call removes in use.

**Our score, applied unchanged as a standalone detector (Figure S2).** For the ceiling comparison we also ran the pipeline on those 100 EEGs with no refitting and no threshold tuning. Against the expert majority the pre-specified score reached **AUROC 0.903 [0.832, 0.958]** for generalized slowing and **0.738** for focal (`results/occasion_model_vs_experts.md`). Predictions were fixed in advance (`docs/phaseA_preregistration.md`); we report the failures below. The focal figure should be read against what the task requires: 21% of the recordings the score must reject as non-focal are EEGs the panel calls generalized-slow, and separating those from focal ones is a topographic judgement that a recording-level spectral deviation cannot make (§3.4c-bis). Morgoth, with full morphological access, is *better* on focal (0.923) than on generalized (0.900); our score is *worse* on focal than on generalized. That asymmetry is the empirical case for the two-stage design.

**Ranking versus calibration are different claims.** The Morgoth gate ranks these EEGs at AUROC 0.923 (focal) / 0.895 (generalized), better than the average expert — but at its shipped threshold it achieves balanced accuracy 0.714 / 0.667, with specificity 1.000 and sensitivity 0.43 / 0.33. Leave-one-out recalibration (no EEG informs its own call) lifts it to 0.845 / 0.814. Our own score behaves the same way: a naive z > 2 cut gives balanced accuracy 0.646, and leave-one-out recalibration lifts it to **0.835**, above the average expert's **0.809**. A system's ranking quality and its operating-point calibration must be reported separately.

**An algorithm can agree with each expert better than experts agree with each other.** If each expert is the latent truth plus independent noise, two experts compound two error sources while an accurate algorithm carries one; classical test theory then predicts κ_ae ≈ √κ_ee for an algorithm at the truth. Recalibrated Morgoth already exceeds the expert–expert ceiling on focal slowing (κ_ae = 0.471 vs κ_ee = 0.403; difference +0.068, 95% CI +0.014 to +0.136). Our score matches it on generalized slowing (κ_ae = 0.459 vs κ_ee = 0.451). Neither reaches √κ_ee (0.63–0.67), so neither sits at the latent truth. Because expert errors are correlated — shared training, shared blind spots — √κ_ee is a conservative target.

**Two pre-registered predictions failed, and both are informative.** First, we predicted our ROC would pass above the mean expert operating point; it does not. At the expert's own specificity (0.884) our sensitivity is **0.684** against their **0.735**. We exceed the average expert on balanced accuracy, but at a different operating point; we do not dominate them. Second, we predicted that restricting scoring to alert (W/N1) segments would beat scoring all stages. It does not (TAR 0.871 vs 0.870; log delta 0.877 vs **0.907**, all-stage winning). Because every segment is already compared to *its own stage's* norm, discarding sleep buys nothing — and the panel read the entire study. What does carry the effect is the **reference population**: holding the score fixed and swapping an alert-routine normal reference for the union reference costs **+0.042 to +0.147** AUROC. The methodological claim is therefore narrower and better supported than the one we set out to make: *the normative reference must be vigilance-matched; the segments need not be restricted to wake.*

**Conspicuity is recoverable where severity was not.** The fraction of the 18 experts who marked slowing is a graded, human, quantitative target. Our deviation score tracks it at Spearman **ρ = 0.652** (p = 2 × 10⁻¹³) for generalized slowing — against ρ = 0.050 for the reader's severity adjective (§3.4b). The measurement is not the problem; the adjective is. This licenses a claim about **how conspicuous** slowing is to trained readers, and no claim about the severity of the underlying pathology.

*One author (M.B.W.) is among the raters in the band-agreement dataset. Excluding that rater changes the reported κ by ≤ 0.005.*

### 3.4e Readers under-report slowing in sleep — a within-subject test

The dose-response (§3.4b) hints that reports under-state slowing, but a hint is not evidence. We tested it directly. Take recordings the reader called abnormal, whose report **names slowing but never mentions sleep**, and which contain scored sleep; ask whether they nonetheless deviate above age- and stage-matched clean-normals **in N2/N3**. The comparison is within-subject — wake and sleep in the same brain — so it cannot be explained by cases being older, sicker or medicated.

They do. Scored against a held-out clean-normal reference, cases' median sleep-stage z is **+0.62** (log delta) and **+0.98** (DAR), against **+0.02** and **−0.03** in held-out controls (n = 686 / 431; Mann–Whitney p < 1e-47).

**The result is only trustworthy because of what threatens it.** Our sleep stager reads the same EEG we score, and pathologically slow *wake* resembles N2 to any stager keyed on slow-wave content. Misstaged slow wake, compared against true-N2 norms, would reproduce the entire finding. Worse, it would also reproduce the within-subject contrast, because misstaging preferentially moves a case's *slowest* wake into the sleep bin. Neither the group difference nor the within-subject gap can discriminate the two explanations.

We therefore adjudicated with a marker that does not depend on delta at all: **sleep spindles** (11–16 Hz sigma activity on central derivations), a physiologic signature of true N2. Spindles are used here to *validate the sleep stage*, not to infer slowing — these are separate findings, separately reported, in clinical practice. Restricting **both** arms to N2 segments carrying a detected spindle, in routine-length recordings, the elevation is undiminished: **AUROC 0.854 [0.797, 0.905]** for log delta and **0.844 [0.787, 0.896]** for DAR, on 88 cases and 123 controls. These are statistically indistinguishable from the same recordings' all-N2 values (0.853 and 0.841): **verifying that the sleep is real does not erode the effect** (**Figure 6**; `results/v4a_wake_sleep.md`). Recruitment met a pre-set bar of ≥60 analysable recordings per arm, and both confidence intervals exclude chance by a wide margin.

Two boundaries. First, spindle verification required locating the analysed clip inside the source recording, which is tractable only for routine-length studies, so the sub-study excludes long-term monitoring; reading EDF durations for the full cohort shows short- and long-recording cases have indistinguishable sleep-stage deviation (log delta +0.609 vs +0.522, p = 0.60), so the restriction does not bound the conclusion. Second, cases' staged N2 is spindle-poorer than controls' (0.40 vs 0.72, p = 2e-6). That is a finding, not evidence: encephalopathy genuinely suppresses spindles, and misstaging would look the same. It is precisely why the analysis is restricted to spindle-*positive* segments rather than resting on spindle counts.

The relative composite (δ+θ)/total is null throughout (AUROC 0.510) — it saturates in N2/N3, where normal relative delta is already high.

**This is the paper's clearest statement of added value.** The reader saw slowing in wake, said nothing about sleep, and the slowing was there — in segments independently confirmed to be sleep. It is not that the model reproduces the report; it is that the model sees what the report omits, in exactly the regime where visual reading is hardest.

### 3.5 Deviation features versus the expert-calibrated Morgoth

Sanity checks confirmed the gate behaves: median Morgoth p_abnormal was 0.10 in normals versus 0.98–0.996 in the focal/generalized groups, p_focal was highest in focal cases, and p_generalized highest in generalized. Our objective, age/sex-deviation logistic regression tracked Morgoth closely (`results/lr_vs_morgoth.md`): for p_abnormal, Pearson r = 0.693 (Spearman ρ = 0.690) with distillation R² = 0.456; for p_slowing, r = 0.635 (ρ = 0.694), R² = 0.458. Against the clinical label directly, our deviation-based LR actually reached AUC 0.962 versus Morgoth 0.921 — our features are highly sensitive to the label — but the correlation of ~0.64–0.69 shows they capture much, though not all, of what the expert-calibrated detector encodes. The probability scatter (**Figure F4**; `figures/lr_vs_morgoth.png`) makes the difference concrete: our LR is over-confident, pushing probabilities toward 0 and 1, whereas the report-calibrated Morgoth is better spread — consistent with high label-sensitivity but only moderate probability agreement, and a reason to keep Morgoth as the gate. ROC and precision–recall curves for abnormal detection by both scores are shown in **Figure F3** (`figures/roc_prc/roc.png`, `figures/roc_prc/prc.png`). Operated as a slowing gate at P ≥ 0.30 (Youden J = 0.75), Morgoth gated in 79% of focal, 86% of generalized, and only 8.9% of normal recordings.

Gate discrimination is **age-dependent** (**Figure F4b**, `results/age_auroc.csv`): abnormal-vs-normal AUROC rises from **0.79 in children (0–12)** to **0.95 in older adults (61–75)**, dipping slightly in the very elderly (0.94 at 76–100); focal detection is strong at all ages (0.97–0.99) while generalized climbs 0.88 → 0.96. The weaker pediatric performance is a concrete reporting caveat — automated slowing detection should be applied more cautiously in children — and reflects both developmental spectral variability and thinner pediatric training coverage.

### 3.6 Agreement with clinical reports

**Label re-derivation.** All recording labels were re-derived from the raw clinical reports plus the structured findings into a single unified table with provenance (`data/derived/labels_unified.parquet`; 12,379 recordings, 93.6% matched to a report). Class assignment reconciles the structured `normal`/`abnormal`/`focal`/`generalized` finding flags with the report text; a normal impression is treated as ground truth, guarded against a data-entry artifact in which "abnormal … due to [findings]" was truncated to "normal" (caught by trusting the independent structured abnormal/focal flags). The clean-normal reference is a reader-normal study with no pathological finding, which may still contain *physiologic* generalized slowing. Critically, the **generalized-slowing class was split into pathologic versus physiologic** slowing: because the raw generalized-slowing flag fires in ~56% of recordings — sweeping in normal drowsy, sleep, and hyperventilation slowing — we labeled ~1,000 stratified cases (large-language-model labeling under a fixed clinical rubric, spot-validated by an expert board-certified neurophysiologist at 15/15 agreement on the hardest cases) and distilled them into a simple, scalable text classifier (TF-IDF + logistic regression; 5-fold CV AUROC 0.960, macro-F1 0.883) that requires no model at inference. Applied cohort-wide, 44% of generalized-slowing mentions are pathologic and 56% physiologic; only the pathologic set defines the `general_slow` class, which sharpened normal-vs-generalized discrimination (TAR whole-head AUROC 0.73→0.81) and roughly doubled the clean-normal reference (2,517→4,869).

We matched cohort recordings to their free-text clinical reports (11,339 / 12,379 matched to report-derived flags; 12,303 / 12,379 matched for text-extracted labels), extracted labels with provenance, and validated (`results/report_agreement.md`; **Table T4**). Raw report text is not published — only derived labels, with pointers back to the source notes. For **report-flag agreement**, Morgoth tracked the reports strongly: abnormal AUC 0.904, focal 0.785, generalized 0.745; our deviation-based LR was close behind at 0.751 / 0.631 / 0.647, establishing face validity. For **report-text agreement** — our generated statement versus the report where the report states the finding — raw agreement was region 0.92 (n = 407), side 0.79 (n = 5,406), and band 0.74 (n = 5,196). **The band figure is withdrawn.** It measures agreement between our regex extractor and the report's band *word*, on one report per recording, and therefore scores text parsing rather than determination of the band from the signal. Scored instead against 18 experts' per-band calls on 1,761 events, with no text extractor in the loop, our band rule is **near-chance**: Cohen κ = 0.070 (focal) and 0.011 (generalized) against expert–expert ceilings of 0.260 and 0.408 (`results/moe_band_vs_ours.md`). Its apparent focal "agreement" of 0.901 is exactly the always-delta base rate (0.902), because 1/f makes raw relative delta exceed relative theta in 98% of segments. Replacing the rule with an age-normalized one — band = argmax(z of relative delta, z of relative theta) against the matched normal reference — raises generalized band agreement to κ = 0.223 but leaves focal unchanged (0.065), and both remain below the expert ceiling. **We therefore do not claim band determination**, and report it as an open problem. The region and side figures are, however, **inflated by class imbalance** and must be read with per-class metrics: 92% of located reports are temporal and 83% of lateralized reports are bilateral, so a system that predicts "temporal/bilateral" by default scores highly on raw agreement while never identifying the other regions (per-class F1 ≈ 0 for frontal/parietal/occipital; left/right F1 0.35/0.24). We therefore evaluated localization honestly with **macro-F1** (equal weight per region) across three predictors (**Table T4b**, `results/region_supervised.md`): the temporal-default macro-F1 = 0.16; a max-deviation-lobe rule = 0.12; and a supervised classifier on age-adjusted per-channel deviations = **0.23** (best), with temporal (F1 0.54) and frontal (0.32) reasonably localizable but parietal/occipital still poor (F1 < 0.1, n ≈ 66 each). Localization is thus **partially solved** — strong for the common temporal/frontal foci, data-limited for rare posterior foci — and is a target for region-stratified data collection rather than a solved axis.

Crucially, **lateralization must be posed as a focal-gated, binary task.** Detection of focal slowing is side-*invariant* (it responds to the *magnitude* of homologous asymmetry), whereas lateralization needs its *sign*; evaluating side as a three-way label over all abnormal recordings is dominated by the bilateral majority (generalized slowing is bilateral by definition), which is why the naive side agreement looked weak. Restricting to focal recordings with a stated side and predicting **left vs. right** from signed homologous asymmetry (`results/lateralization_gated.md`, **Figure F4d**) gives **AUROC 0.866** (balanced accuracy 0.81; n = 555), with a single signed feature — temporal δ asymmetry — already at 0.856. Lateralization is therefore a *strong* axis once gated correctly; the operational design is to predict side only when the gate calls focal, and to abstain to "bilateral/uncertain" when the asymmetry magnitude is small. Lateralization is also **band-matched**: theta-predominant focal slowing is lateralized by theta asymmetry (AUROC 0.91) and delta by delta (0.90), with both for mixed — so the reported side is driven by the same band as the reported slowing. For **generalized** slowing (no side), an **anterior-vs-posterior** predominance axis (frontal FIRDA-like vs posterior OIRDA-like) is recoverable at AUROC ~0.66 (`results/region_gated.md`). Focal **lobe** localization, evaluated focal-gated, is strong for temporal (F1 0.64) but data-limited for rarer frontal/central/posterior foci. Band was initially the weak axis (0.33): recognizing that pathological slowing is usually *mixed* and defaulting to mixed unless one band clearly dominates raised band agreement from 0.33 to 0.74, while side and region were already strong. Band now appears near its ceiling for spectral-only features — 0.74 approaches the ~74% base rate of "mixed" slowing in the reports, so a band-power model that defaults to mixed can gain little more without morphology. A pilot morphology feature (slow-band spectral centroid) did not improve the band call (0.45 vs 0.74) because the centroid is dominated by the 1/f background (~2.1 Hz for both delta and mixed segments); aperiodic (FOOOF) decomposition is the indicated next step (`docs/morphology_features.md`). All three axes now have good face validity against experts, and all labels (Morgoth probabilities, report-derived flags, and report-text-extracted band/side/region) are published per-recording with provenance.

### 3.7 Example generated reports — and what we deliberately do not say

The gated pipeline (`results/example_reports_final.md`; gate at Morgoth P(slowing) ≥ 0.302) emits a clinician-style sentence built from the gate's *whether/what* decision and our normative *description*. The sentences below reflect the validation in §3.4b–§3.4e, which forced us to **remove** three elements an earlier draft included.

> *Left temporal slowing, present in 48% of alert segments; 2.1 SD above age- and stage-matched norms (94th centile of the clinician-normal distribution); longest run 3.8 min over 4 episodes.* (age 50 F, focal)

> *Generalized slowing, present in 19% of segments; 1.3 SD above age- and stage-matched norms; longest run 0.7 min over 6 episodes; **present only during sleep**, accentuated in N1.* (age 38 F, generalized)

> *Left parasagittal slowing, 2.3 SD above age- and stage-matched norms; accentuated in N3.* (age 71 M, focal)

> *Generalized slowing, present in 57% of segments; **present only during sleep**, accentuated in N2.* (age 81 F, generalized)

**What was removed, and why.** *(i) The severity adjective* ("mild", "moderate", "marked"). Our score does not recover it under any of 168 feature × statistic × normalization × stratum combinations (§3.4b), and the panel data explain why: a reader re-reading the same EEG reproduces their own slowing call at κ = 0.56–0.64. We report a **deviation in SD and a centile against the clinician-normal distribution** instead — a measurement, whose value does not depend on whether a reader noticed it. *(ii) The band* ("theta/delta", "mixed"). Against 18 experts' per-band calls our rule is near-chance (κ 0.01–0.07), and its apparent agreement was the always-delta base rate (§3.6). We say "slowing" and stop. *(iii) The peak statistic.* "Peak N SD" was a maximum over hundreds of segments and is dominated by the noisiest one (observed maxima > 19 SD are artifact); it is replaced by a robust upper quantile.

**What was kept, and on what evidence.** Localization by side and lobe (focal lateralization AUROC 0.87, band-matched; temporal F1 0.64), prevalence and persistence as descriptive counts, the deviation magnitude — which tracks **how many of 18 experts see the slowing** at ρ = 0.652 (§3.4d) — and, most consequentially, **stage-accentuation and sleep-only presence**, which §3.4e establishes on spindle-verified sleep. The phrase *"present only during sleep"* is the one clause in these sentences that a clinical report systematically omits, and it is the one we can now defend.

## 4. Discussion

**What is new.** This work unifies four previously separate literatures into a single instrument. Unlike lifespan qEEG that reports *normal values*, we score *deviation from normal* per recording. Unlike the classic norms (Petersén & Eeg-Olofsson; John et al.), which are small-N, wake-only, age-banded, and — in the commercial lineage — opaque, our norms are lifespan-continuous, sex-specific, sleep-stage-resolved, built at the scale of ~12,000 patients, and fully reproducible in open Python. Unlike disease-specific abnormality indices (BSI, DAR, ICU signatures), which use fixed thresholds tied to one setting and ignore age/sex/stage, our features are normed against the matched normal population. And unlike binary classifiers (TUAB lineage) or ungrounded report generators, we produce localized, banded, graded, stage-aware descriptions validated against the actual report corpus. This extends the deviation idea of John et al. (1980/1988) to a modern scale and to slowing and asymmetry specifically, and it is the functional-EEG analog of the MRI "brain charts" of Bethlehem et al. (2022).

**Clinical implications.** The pipeline offers an objective, reproducible second read and a draft report. Its strongest axes are **whether/what** (abnormal detection, and band once defaulted to mixed) and **grading against age/stage-matched norms**; a handful of interpretable features (TAR, DAR, delta/theta power, homologous asymmetry) carry most of the discriminative signal. **Localization is more nuanced than raw agreement suggests** — the headline region/side numbers are inflated by the temporal/bilateral majority, and honest per-class evaluation (macro-F1 0.23) shows reliable localization only for the common temporal and frontal foci, with posterior foci data-limited — so topographic claims in the generated report should be surfaced with a confidence that reflects this. Stage-resolved norms let the system distinguish physiological sleep slowing from pathology automatically, something wake-only norms cannot do and a frequent source of over-reading. The published per-recording labels also constitute a reusable resource for benchmarking.

**Reports are a directionally biased reference, and a normative model can detect what they miss.** Clinical EEG reports are our reference standard, but they are an *imperfect reference with a known, directional bias*: because theta/delta slowing is a normal feature of sleep and "how much is too much" is hard to judge by eye, neurophysiologists systematically **under-declare slowing that occurs in sleep**, and generalized slowing is under-called relative to focal (in our report corpus, generalized-slowing sentences are ~1.5× more likely than focal to be qualified as drowsy/sleep-related, and a study with genuinely pathological sleep slowing is often signed out "normal overall"). We therefore treat concordance with reports as validation only *where reports are reliable* (focal, and awake findings), and pose a second, complementary hypothesis: that our **age × sex × stage** normative model is itself the better instrument in exactly the regime humans find hardest — generalized slowing in sleep — because it applies a population-calibrated threshold *per stage* (the normal band in N2/N3 is high and wide, so only excess beyond it is flagged). Because no independent gold standard exists, we make the "excess" detections credible three ways rather than one: **(1) dose-response** — deviation magnitude should rise monotonically across report strata (clean-normal < abnormal-unspecified < abnormal-with-slowing-named); **(2) a within-subject wake→sleep test** — studies whose report flags abnormality *in wake only* should also show elevated deviation *in their sleep stages*, evidence that the abnormality is present in sleep and detected there even when the reader did not call it; and **(3) convergent validity** — deviation should track independent abnormality markers (absent sleep spindles/K-complexes, abnormal posterior dominant rhythm, and the Morgoth gate). We frame human-missed detections as *candidate* abnormalities supported by this convergent evidence, and propose the definitive test — a blinded expert re-read of high-deviation report-normal sleep studies (or linkage to clinical outcomes) — as prospective validation. This reframes the contribution from "reproduce the report" to "a stage-aware normative complement that is most valuable where expert reading is weakest" (full plan in `docs/normative_deviation_plan.md`).

**Laterality of focal slowing — an age-dependent left predominance.** Among focal recordings with a stated side, the reported side is **left in ~74% overall, rising with age from ~0.65 (≤18 y) to ~0.77 (≥76 y)** — i.e., the well-described left-anterior-temporal predominance of intermittent temporal slowing is concentrated in older adults, consistent with the "temporal slowing of the elderly"/benign-aging literature and its proposed cerebrovascular basis (Inui et al., *Psychiatry Clin Neurosci* 2001; Meritam Larsen & Beniczky systematic review, *Seizure* 2025; Wüstenhagen et al. SCORE normal-variants, *Clin Neurophysiol Pract* 2022; older community cohorts Hughes & Cayaffa 1977, Oken & Kaye 1992). Whether this skew is fully biological versus partly methodological (montage/reference conventions, side-asymmetric artifact, greater scrutiny of the left anterior temporal chain) is unresolved and we do not adjudicate it. Two design consequences follow. First, because we want the system to report *whatever side is actually present* without inheriting this prevalence prior, we train and evaluate the lateralizer with **left↔right symmetry augmentation** (equivalently, an intercept-free antisymmetric model on signed asymmetry), which removes the prior while preserving discrimination (left/right recall equalized, AUROC unchanged; flip-consistency exact). Second, lateralization is driven by *within-subject* signed asymmetry, which is intrinsically age- and sex-controlled — and empirically its AUROC is stable across the lifespan (0.81–0.89) — so, unlike detection and regional deviation (both explicitly age×sex×stage-normed), the lateralizer needs no separate age model.

**Relationship to Morgoth.** The gate-and-describe design deliberately separates *detection* (Morgoth, calibrated to expert calls but unable to describe) from *description* (our interpretable, sensitive, but not-report-calibrated norms). The two are complementary: Morgoth decides whether and what to report, our features supply the adjectives and the quantitative parenthetical, and disagreement between them (e.g., Morgoth-focal with asymmetry z ≈ 0) is itself an actionable low-confidence flag. That some Morgoth-confident cases have flat spectral features points to the main descriptive limitation: our current features are spectral, and Morgoth evidently exploits morphological patterns (waveform shape, transients, rhythmicity) that band power does not capture. A first pilot in this direction — a slow-band spectral centroid intended to sharpen the band call — was negative (0.45 vs 0.74), because the centroid is dominated by the 1/f background (~2.1 Hz for both delta and mixed segments) rather than by any oscillatory peak. The lesson is that closing the gap needs features that separate periodic from aperiodic activity: aperiodic (FOOOF) exponent/offset, time-domain morphology (line length, amplitude, Hjorth parameters), and rhythmicity measures that distinguish polymorphic from rhythmic (FIRDA/TIRDA) slowing, together with distillation of Morgoth probabilities into interpretable features. This roadmap is scoped and prioritized in `docs/morphology_features.md`.

## 5. Limitations

Several limitations temper these results. **Absolute relative-power calibration** is approximate; although the delta band was recalibrated to 1–4 Hz (normal whole-head relative delta 0.34) and all z-based results are scale-invariant, raw relative-power magnitudes and cross-feature absolute comparisons should be read cautiously. **Band determination is unsolved, and we say so.** Against 18 experts' per-band calls our rule is near-chance (κ 0.01–0.07); the previously reported 0.74 was agreement with the report's band *word* via a regex, not determination of the band from the signal (§3.6). Experts themselves agree on band poorly (κ 0.09–0.38), so the ceiling is low — but our current rule does not approach even that. An age-normalized rule helps for generalized slowing (κ 0.223) and not for focal. Finer band attribution will require morphology and better spectral resolution. **Morphology is not yet modeled** — a known cause of weak descriptions in some Morgoth-confident cases. **Region localization is only partially solved.** Honest per-class evaluation (macro-F1 0.23 for the best, supervised, predictor) shows reliable localization for temporal and frontal foci but weak performance for the rarer central/parietal/occipital foci (per-class F1 < 0.1, n ≈ 66 each); the high raw region-agreement (0.92) is a majority-class artifact of temporal predominance. Improving this needs both better features and **region-stratified data collection** to build an adequately powered off-temporal evaluation and training set.

**Coverage is uneven, and one gap was self-inflicted and now fixed.** Sleep staging in the source cohort had originally been run only on the normal recordings, so abnormal-with-sleep cells were empty and stage-stratified abnormal analyses were initially impossible; we corrected this by staging the abnormal recordings directly from their archived raw clips (`scripts/36`), and the stage-stratified analysis (§3.3) now uses real staged focal/generalized cases. Adult N3 remains the thinnest normal cell (wide CIs). Both gaps are further addressed by the validated full-recording ingestion pipeline (`io/edf.py` harmonization + `features/artifact.py` rejection + whole-recording staging and per-stage features), which folds in longer/overnight recordings from the broader repository; a cloud-scale run is prepared (`fleet/`, `docs/aws_cloud_plan.md`). **Single health system** limits generalizability; external validation is needed. **Label quality required active correction, and one concern remains open.** Auditing the labels during analysis revealed three problems, two now fixed: (i) the *normal reference was 57% contaminated* by recordings that also carried a slowing flag, because a priority cascade let "normal" win ties — corrected to a clean-normal reference (slowing/abnormal override normal), which lowered the normal whole-head relative-delta baseline (≈0.41→0.35) and makes every deviation score honest; (ii) the *report-text side/region extractor was badly under-calling laterality* — it scoped only to sentences containing "slow," checked "diffuse/generalized" before any explicit side, and ignored electrode names, dumping ~81% of slowing reports into "bilateral"; a rewrite (per-clause scoping, 10-20 electrode→side/region mapping, R>L/L>R predominance, side-wins-over-diffuse) roughly tripled recoverable unilateral sides and raised the focal lateralization set from ~555 to ~1,300–2,000 recordings (AUROC 0.87→0.93). The **open concern** is the *generalized-slowing flag itself*: it is positive in ~77% of the cohort, implausibly high for pathological generalized slowing, and almost certainly sweeps in physiologic/drowsy sleep slowing; because it is a whole-recording, stage-agnostic label it cannot by itself distinguish pathological from physiologic slowing — which is precisely why we lean on the per-stage normative model and the convergent-validity strategy above rather than on this flag as ground truth. **Report text is an imperfect and *directionally biased* ground truth** more broadly (see Discussion): completeness and phrasing vary, and sleep/generalized slowing is systematically under-reported, bounding — and in the sleep regime, likely *under*-stating — achievable agreement. Finally, **routine EEG is short**, limiting sleep capture and the stability of stage-specific per-recording estimates.

**Report-to-recording pairing is heuristic, and severity grading is a negative result.** The reports were joined to EEGs at the patient level before we received them: one report is stamped onto a mean of 2.9 (maximum 170) of that patient's EEGs, so 17.2% of our recordings carry text describing a different study of the same patient (§2.6). We repair this by assigning each report to the EEG nearest it in time, but the available timing field is an order-to-study offset (median |Δ| = 14.2 h even for unambiguously-owned reports), so **"cleanly paired" means probably, not certainly, the right report**; a definitive mapping needs a true report↔study key from the source repository. Detection is insensitive to this (all AUROCs move within their bootstrap CIs), because a borrowed report belongs to the same patient and abnormality is stable across a patient's studies. **Severity grading is not**, and we could not recover the reader's mild/moderate/marked adjective under any of 168 feature × statistic × normalization × stratum combinations (best |ρ| = 0.179, failing Bonferroni, wrong sign; §3.4b). The expert panel (§3.x) shows the adjective is attached to a judgement of low reliability, which bounds how strongly any measurement could correlate with it; we report the null and do not claim a severity scale. Two further defects are disclosed rather than fixed: the per-stage "best feature" AUROCs were originally selected and reported on the same data. Nested cross-validation (5-fold outer × 5 repeats, split on patient, with the feature chosen in an inner loop and the normal reference re-estimated per fold) shows the optimism is negligible: W 0.848→0.856, N1 0.875→0.871, N2 0.791→0.795, N3 0.758→0.751, REM 0.825→0.825, every published value inside the nested confidence interval (`results/nested_cv_detection.md`). Feature selection contributes ≤0.004 AUROC, and selection is stable (W→TAR and N2/N3→DAR unanimous across 25 folds).

## 6. Conclusion

We present, to our knowledge, the first large-scale, lifespan- and sleep-stage-resolved, deviation-from-normal instrument for EEG slowing. Built on 12,379 routine clinical EEGs from ~12,000 patients, it reproduces known development and aging, quantifies the stage-dependence of physiological slowing (W ≈ N1 < N2 < N3), identifies a compact interpretable feature set (TAR, DAR, delta/theta power, homologous asymmetry), tracks an expert-calibrated foundation-model detector (r ≈ 0.64–0.69), and produces clinician-style report sentences that agree with free-text reports on region (0.92), side (0.79), and band (0.74). By coupling normative deviation scoring to a foundation-model gate and validating the output in the language of clinical reports, it moves beyond both un-normed abnormality indices and ungrounded automated interpretation. We release an open, reproducible Python package and a published per-recording label set to enable external validation and extension, particularly to adult deep-sleep norms and morphology-aware features.

## Data and code availability

Code is released as the open-source package `bdsp-core/morgoth-slowing-growth-curves`, with a numbered, end-to-end reproducible pipeline (`03 features → 04 curves → 06 discrimination → 09 map-stages → 10 stage-curves → 11 descriptive → 13 recompute → 14 gate → 15 feature-select → 16 gated report`). We publish per-recording labels — Morgoth probabilities, report-derived normal/abnormal and focal/generalized flags, and report-text-extracted band/side/region — each with provenance pointing back to the source note, together with the growth-curve tables and cohort metadata. Raw EEG and free-text report content are not redistributed; they are available via BDSP credentialed access. [TBD: DOI / repository release version and license confirmation before submission.]
